# Logic Resources

## Knowledge

- [forall x: Calgary — A Free and Open Introduction to Formal Logic](https://forallx.openlogicproject.org/)
  Full, rigorous, free textbook: propositional logic (TFL), predicate logic (FOL) with identity, natural deduction proofs, soundness, and a modal logic chapter. Use for: the primary spine of this entire topic — nearly every lesson should cite it.

- [forall x — original by P.D. Magnus](https://www.fecundity.com/logic/)
  The original OER this topic forked from. Use for: alternate explanations/exercises if the Calgary edition is unclear on something.

- [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Informal Logic](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-informal/)
  Rigorous survey of informal logic, argumentation theory, and the fallacies tradition (Hamblin, Toulmin). Use for: informal fallacies, real-world argument evaluation, why formal validity isn't the whole story.

- [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Classical Logic](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/logic-classical/)
  Formal treatment of classical propositional and predicate logic. Use for: precise definitions when forallx moves too fast.

- [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy — Gödel's Incompleteness Theorems](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/goedel-incompleteness/)
  Authoritative source on what the incompleteness theorems actually say (and the popular misconceptions about them). Use for: the modern-theory capstone lesson.

## Wisdom (Communities)

- [r/logic](https://www.reddit.com/r/logic/)
  Small but technically serious community for formal logic questions. Use for: checking proof attempts, notation questions.

- [r/PhilosophyOfScience and r/askphilosophy](https://www.reddit.com/r/askphilosophy/)
  Well-moderated, academic-leaning. Use for: how professional philosophers actually evaluate arguments in debates.
