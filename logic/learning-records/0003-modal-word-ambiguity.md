# Insight: Caught Modal-Word Ambiguity in Atomic Propositions

User independently noticed that "Ticket → Enter" was ambiguous between "having a ticket implies you actually entered" and "having a ticket implies you may enter" — correctly identifying that the lesson's quiz sloppily let `Enter` stand in for a permission claim ("may enter") without ever pinning that down. Verified the correct reading themselves before I confirmed it: the sentence is about permission/eligibility, not a physical event, and the naive "actually entered" reading makes the symbolization factually wrong (a ticket doesn't force you to walk in).

**Implications:** This is well above the expected floor for lesson 2 — recognizing that modal words (may/can/must/should) change what an atomic proposition is claiming, and that symbolization errors can live in the proposition's definition rather than the connective choice, is exactly the kind of care lesson 10 (Modal Logic) exists to formalize. Can move through remaining propositional/predicate lessons at a faster clip and lean into this kind of precision-first framing rather than re-explaining it. Worth flagging modal words explicitly whenever they show up in future symbolization exercises rather than assuming they're transparent.
