Arguments, Validity & Soundness

Before any symbols, before ∧ and ∀, there is one distinction that everything else in this topic is built on. Get this wrong and no amount of formal notation will save you. Get it right and you can evaluate an argument on any subject — politics, science, religion, a conversation at dinner — without knowing anything about the subject itself.

What an argument actually is

In logic, an argument is not a fight. It's a specific structure: one or more premises (claims offered as reasons) leading to a conclusion (the claim they're meant to support).

Example argument Premise 1: All humans are mortal.
Premise 2: Socrates is a human.
Conclusion: Therefore, Socrates is mortal.

That's it. That's the whole unit. Every debate you've ever heard is, underneath the rhetoric, a bundle of these — usually several, chained together, often with premises left unstated.

The distinction that changes everything: validity

Here is the single most important idea in this entire topic:

Validity is about form, not content An argument is valid if the conclusion must be true whenever all the premises are true. Validity says nothing about whether the premises actually are true. It only asks: does the conclusion follow from them?

This is deeply counterintuitive at first, so sit with an uncomfortable example:

Valid — but with a false premise Premise 1: All birds can fly.
Premise 2: Penguins are birds.
Conclusion: Therefore, penguins can fly.

This argument is perfectly valid. The structure is airtight — if both premises were true, the conclusion would have to follow. The problem isn't the logic. The problem is that Premise 1 is false (penguins exist). This is the move most people miss in a live debate: they attack the conclusion when they should be attacking a premise, or they attack a premise when the real problem is the structure.

Soundness: validity plus truth

A sound argument is a valid argument where all the premises are actually true. Soundness is the thing you actually want when you're trying to establish that a conclusion is correct — validity alone only guarantees internal consistency.

Deductive vs inductive

Everything above describes deductive arguments — the kind where the premises are supposed to guarantee the conclusion. Most real-world arguments (scientific, everyday, political) are inductive — the premises make the conclusion probable, not certain.

  • Deductive: "All men are mortal. Socrates is a man. Therefore Socrates is mortal." — certainty, if premises hold.
  • Inductive: "The sun has risen every day recorded in human history. Therefore the sun will rise tomorrow." — strong support, but not certainty.

Inductive arguments are evaluated on a different scale — strength, not validity. A strong inductive argument makes its conclusion likely; it can never make it certain the way a valid deductive argument does. Confusing the two is one of the most common moves — sloppy or deliberate — in real debates: treating a merely probable inductive case as if it had deductive certainty.

Why this is the whole game

When you listen to a debate from now on, you're doing two separable jobs:

  1. Structural check: does the conclusion actually follow from the premises (validity), or is there a gap?
  2. Factual check: are the premises actually true (soundness)?

People conflate these constantly. Someone who says "that argument is wrong" is usually pointing at one or the other without saying which — and the two require completely different kinds of pushback. If the structure is broken, no amount of true premises fixes it. If the structure is fine but a premise is false, the argument's logic isn't the problem.

Practice before next lesson

Pick a debate you've actually watched or listened to recently. Isolate one argument from it — just one premise-conclusion chain — and write it out in the form: "Premise 1... Premise 2... Therefore...". Then ask yourself the two-question test: is it valid? Are the premises true? You'll likely find the structure was never made explicit in the original debate at all — that's normal. Making it explicit is the whole skill.
