Propositional Logic: Syntax & Connectives

Lesson 1 gave you validity and soundness, but only in prose — "if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true." That's fine for simple syllogisms, but it falls apart fast on anything with nested conditions, "unless," "neither/nor," or multi-step reasoning. Propositional logic gives you a precise, unambiguous language to represent exactly what an argument's structure is, stripped of English's vagueness.

Quick recall

Before continuing — from memory:

  • What's the difference between validity and soundness?
  • Can a valid argument have a false conclusion?

Answers: validity = structure guarantees truth-preservation; soundness = valid + true premises · Yes — if at least one premise is false, a valid argument can still have a false conclusion.

Atomic propositions

The basic unit is the atomic proposition — a statement that is either true or false, with no internal logical structure. We name them with capital letters: P, Q, R...

Propositions "It is raining." "Socrates is mortal." "2 + 2 = 5." All propositions — each is true or false (even if we don't know which yet).
Not propositions "Close the door." (command) · "Is it raining?" (question) · "Wow!" (exclamation) — none of these are true or false, so they have no place in propositional logic on their own.

The five connectives

You already know these — they're boolean operators. If you've written if p && q in Rust or C, you've used propositional logic without the name.

SymbolNameEnglishRust / C
¬PNegation"not P"!p
P ∧ QConjunction"P and Q"p && q
P ∨ QDisjunction"P or Q" (inclusive)p || q
P → QConditional"if P then Q"!p || q
P ↔ QBiconditional"P if and only if Q"p == q
The one that isn't obvious: ∨ is inclusive "P ∨ Q" is true if P is true, Q is true, or both. This matches Rust/C's || exactly — not the everyday English "or" that often means "one or the other, not both" (an exclusive or, like a restaurant menu's "soup or salad"). Logic always means inclusive ∨ unless stated otherwise.
Antecedent and consequent In P → Q, P is the antecedent (the "if" part) and Q is the consequent (the "then" part). These names keep coming up — the fallacy affirming the consequent mistakes "Q is true" for proof that "P is true"; denying the antecedent mistakes "P is false" for proof that "Q is false." Both are covered properly in lesson 12 (Informal Fallacies).

Well-formed formulas (WFFs)

Not every string of symbols is a legal formula — just like not every sequence of tokens compiles. Propositional logic has a grammar that says precisely which strings count. Here it is again, this time unpacked piece by piece.

φ ::= P | Q | R | ...        // any atomic proposition is a WFF
    | ¬φ                       // negating a WFF gives a WFF
    | (φ ∧ φ)                  // conjoining two WFFs, in parens, is a WFF
    | (φ ∨ φ)
    | (φ → φ)
    | (φ ↔ φ)

Reading the notation: φ ("phi") is a placeholder — it doesn't name one specific formula, it stands for "any WFF, whatever that turns out to be." ::= means "can be defined as," and | separates alternatives, the same job | does in a Rust match pattern. Read the whole block as: "a WFF is either an atomic proposition, or a negation of a WFF, or a conjunction of two WFFs, or..." — six ways to build one, and five of the six require you to already have smaller WFFs in hand. That's what makes it recursive.

The Rust translation This grammar is a recursive enum: 
enum Formula {
    Atom(char),                              // P | Q | R | ...
    Not(Box<Formula>),                       // ¬φ
    And(Box<Formula>, Box<Formula>),          // (φ ∧ φ)
    Or(Box<Formula>, Box<Formula>),
    Implies(Box<Formula>, Box<Formula>),
    Iff(Box<Formula>, Box<Formula>),
}
Box is doing the same job the recursion in the grammar does: a Formula can contain other Formulas, so you need indirection just to describe the type. Atom is the base case — no Formula inside — exactly matching the grammar's first line, the only alternative that doesn't mention φ on the right.

Building a formula step by step

Take ((P ∧ Q) → (¬R ∨ S)). Nobody writes this in one move — it gets certified well-formed by applying the grammar's rules bottom-up, smallest pieces first:

  1. P is a WFF — atomic (base case).
  2. Q is a WFF — atomic.
  3. (P ∧ Q) is a WFF — conjunction rule, applied to steps 1 and 2.
  4. R is a WFF — atomic.
  5. ¬R is a WFF — negation rule, applied to step 4.
  6. S is a WFF — atomic.
  7. (¬R ∨ S) is a WFF — disjunction rule, applied to steps 5 and 6.
  8. ((P ∧ Q) → (¬R ∨ S)) is a WFF — conditional rule, applied to steps 3 and 7. This step was applied last, so → is the main connective.

Main connective

Every non-atomic WFF has exactly one main connective — highlighted at the root of the tree above. It's whichever connective was applied last when the formula was built, and it determines the formula's overall shape: everything else is detail buried inside its left and/or right branch.

Example (P ∧ Q) → R — despite the ∧ inside, the main connective is →. The whole formula is a conditional whose antecedent happens to be a conjunction. Read it as: "IF (P and Q), THEN R" — not "P AND (Q implies R)".

The conditional's uncomfortable truth

This is the part that trips up almost everyone the first time, and it matters enormously for evaluating real arguments.

P → Q is true whenever P is false — regardless of Q "If the moon is made of cheese, then 2 + 2 = 4" is, by the material conditional, a true statement. It sounds absurd in English, but it's exactly what !p || q evaluates to when p is false: always true. A false antecedent makes the whole conditional vacuously true.

Why does this matter for debates? Because people routinely build arguments on conditionals whose antecedent is false, then act as if that makes the conditional meaningful evidence for something. A conditional being true tells you nothing about whether its antecedent or consequent is true — that distinction (validity of the conditional structure vs. truth of its parts) is precisely lesson 1's validity/soundness split, now showing up inside a single connective.

Symbolizing English

Certain English phrases map to specific connectives — this is the translation skill you'll use constantly:

English phraseConnectiveNote
"P unless Q"P ∨ Q"unless" ≈ "or"
"if P, then Q"P → Qthe plain form
"P only if Q"P → Qsame as "if P then Q" — just reworded
"P if Q"Q → Preversed — the missing "only" flips direction
"neither P nor Q"¬P ∧ ¬Qequivalently ¬(P ∨ Q)
"P unless not Q"P ∨ ¬Qjust substitute directly
"Only if" vs "if" — one word flips the direction "If P then Q" and "P only if Q" are the same statement, both P → Q. The trap is "P if Q" — drop the word "only" and the direction reverses to Q → P. Concretely: "you'll pass only if you study" means Pass → Study (passing guarantees you studied). "You'll pass if you study" means Study → Pass (studying guarantees you'll pass) — a completely different, much stronger claim. Same two words, reordered, opposite direction.
The falsification test — a check that always works Recall: P → Q is broken by exactly one scenario — P happens, Q doesn't. So ask: what single scenario would prove "you'll pass only if you study" was a lie? Answer: passing without studying. That's the only thing that breaks it — so P = Pass, Q = Study, giving Pass → Study.

Now test the tempting wrong answer, Study → Pass. That gets broken by studying but not passing. But does failing after studying make the original sentence a lie? No — "only if you study" never promised studying would work, only that passing requires it. Since that scenario doesn't break the sentence, Study → Pass isn't what it means. When in doubt, find the one scenario that would make the English sentence false, then match it to the one scenario that makes P → Q false.
Pin down what your atomic proposition actually means Watch for modal words like "may," "can," "must," "should" — they change what the proposition is claiming. "You may enter only if you have a ticket" is a statement about permission, not about whether you physically walked through the door. If you sloppily let Enter mean "you actually entered," the sentence "you may enter if you have a ticket" would wrongly symbolize as Ticket → Entered — claiming a ticket guarantees you enter, which is false; you could have a ticket and simply not go in. The proposition has to mean "you are permitted to enter" for the conditional to hold at all. Before symbolizing any sentence, state in your own words exactly what each atomic proposition claims — a sloppily-defined proposition produces a wrong formula even when you pick the right connective. This is exactly why lesson 10 (Modal Logic) gives "may" and "must" their own dedicated symbols instead of folding them into plain propositions.
Necessary ≠ sufficient — the ticket, precisely Even with Permitted pinned down correctly, one trap remains: "only if" claims a necessary condition, not a sufficient one — and those are genuinely different claims. Necessary: a ticket is required for permission. Sufficient: a ticket alone guarantees permission. "Only if you have a ticket" promises the first, not the second.

Test it: imagine a sold-out venue. You have a valid ticket, but security turns you away — the room is over capacity. Here Ticket = true, Permitted = false. Does that break "you may enter only if you have a ticket"? No — nobody without a ticket got in either; the rule is still honestly enforced. Since this scenario doesn't falsify the English sentence, Ticket → Permitted was never guaranteed by it. Check Permitted → Ticket against the same scenario: untouched, since nobody was permitted without a ticket — which is exactly why that's the correct symbolization, not the other way around.
