Logical Equivalence & Normal Forms

You already proved one equivalence by hand, back at the start of this topic: P → Q ≡ ¬P ∨ Q. This lesson gives you the full toolkit of equivalences — the rewrite rules that let you take any formula and reshape it into something logically identical but structurally different. Then it gives you two standard target shapes every formula can be rewritten into: CNF and DNF.

Quick recall

From lesson 3: two formulas are logically equivalent exactly when their truth tables match on every row. That's the test everything below is built on — every law in this lesson can be verified that same way.

The equivalence laws

These are the standard rewrite rules of propositional logic. Each one is provable by truth table — you're not asked to take any of these on faith.

NameLaw
Double Negation¬¬P ≡ P
De Morgan (¬∧)¬(P ∧ Q) ≡ ¬P ∨ ¬Q
De Morgan (¬∨)¬(P ∨ Q) ≡ ¬P ∧ ¬Q
CommutativityP ∧ Q ≡ Q ∧ P  ·  P ∨ Q ≡ Q ∨ P
Associativity(P∧Q)∧R ≡ P∧(Q∧R)  ·  same for ∨
DistributivityP∧(Q∨R) ≡ (P∧Q)∨(P∧R)  ·  and the mirror
IdempotenceP ∧ P ≡ P  ·  P ∨ P ≡ P
IdentityP ∧ ⊤ ≡ P  ·  P ∨ ⊥ ≡ P
DominationP ∨ ⊤ ≡ ⊤  ·  P ∧ ⊥ ≡ ⊥
Material ImplicationP → Q ≡ ¬P ∨ Q
ContrapositionP → Q ≡ ¬Q → ¬P
Biconditional ExpansionP ↔ Q ≡ (P → Q) ∧ (Q → P)
Material Implication — where you started That very first row is the equivalence you derived by hand at the beginning of this topic (P → Q ≡ ¬P ∨ Q) — now filed properly among its siblings. Everything in this table is the same kind of move: a formula that looks different but is truth-functionally identical, in every possible world.
The Rust translation — De Morgan's laws You've almost certainly written De Morgan's laws in code without naming them: !(a && b) == (!a || !b) and !(a || b) == (!a && !b). This is the exact same law — negating a conjunction flips it into a disjunction of negations, and vice versa. Compilers and linters use this to simplify conditions; you can use it to simplify arguments.

Normal forms: CNF and DNF

A literal is an atomic proposition or its negation — P or ¬P, nothing more complex. Two standard shapes are built entirely from literals combined with just ∧ and ∨:

Conjunctive Normal Form (CNF) An AND of ORs: (l₁ ∨ l₂ ∨ ...) ∧ (l₃ ∨ l₄ ∨ ...) ∧ ... — a conjunction of "clauses," each clause a disjunction of literals.
Disjunctive Normal Form (DNF) An OR of ANDs: (l₁ ∧ l₂ ∧ ...) ∨ (l₃ ∧ l₄ ∨ ...) ∨ ... — the mirror image of CNF.

Every formula in propositional logic can be rewritten into CNF, and separately into DNF. Neither is "more true" than the original — they're just standardized shapes, useful because algorithms can process a standard shape far more easily than arbitrary nested formulas.

Why this matters beyond logic class CNF is the input format every SAT solver expects — the algorithms (DPLL, CDCL) that decide whether a formula is satisfiable, which underlie automated theorem proving, hardware/software verification, and scheduling/constraint solvers. If you've ever seen a `.cnf` file or heard "reduce to SAT," this is the format. Digital circuit minimization (turning a boolean expression into the fewest logic gates) uses the same normal-form machinery.

Converting to CNF — worked example

Convert ¬(P → Q) ∨ R into CNF, one law at a time:

  1. Eliminate → using Material Implication: ¬(P → Q) ∨ R becomes ¬(¬P ∨ Q) ∨ R
  2. Push ¬ inward using De Morgan: ¬(¬P ∨ Q) becomes (¬¬P ∧ ¬Q), which simplifies via Double Negation to (P ∧ ¬Q). Formula so far: (P ∧ ¬Q) ∨ R
  3. Distribute ∨ over ∧ — CNF needs ANDs of ORs, not ORs of ANDs, so distribute: (P ∧ ¬Q) ∨ R becomes (P ∨ R) ∧ (¬Q ∨ R)

Final CNF: (P ∨ R) ∧ (¬Q ∨ R) — two clauses, each a disjunction of literals, joined by ∧. Let's verify the whole conversion actually preserved meaning, start to finish:

Converting to DNF is the mirror process: eliminate →, push ¬ inward with De Morgan, then distribute ∧ over ∨ instead of ∨ over ∧.
