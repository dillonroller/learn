Natural Deduction: Propositional Proofs

Truth tables always work, but they don't scale and they don't explain anything — a 6-atom argument needs 64 rows, and even when it's valid, the table doesn't tell you why. Natural deduction is the alternative: a small set of rules for chaining premises into a conclusion, one clearly-justified step at a time. This is what an actual proof looks like, and it's the same reasoning skeleton a strong debate argument follows.

Quick recall

From lesson 3: an argument is valid exactly when no row makes the premises true and the conclusion false. From lesson 4: two formulas are interchangeable wherever one appears, if they're logically equivalent.

Why proofs, not just truth tables

This mirrors a real distinction in software verification: model checking exhaustively tests every possible state (like a truth table — sound, but explodes combinatorially), while theorem proving builds a short chain of justified steps (like natural deduction — scales to arbitrary complexity, and the proof itself explains the reasoning). Both prove validity. Only one of them reads like an argument a human actually makes.

Core inference rules

Each rule below takes one or two already-established formulas and licenses a new one. Every rule here is itself just a valid argument form — you can verify any of them with a truth table, the same way lesson 3 verified Modus Ponens.

What ⊢ actually means The turnstile (⊢) is not a connective like ∧ or → — it's a claim about an argument, not a formula inside one. "Premises ⊢ Conclusion" reads "the conclusion is derivable from the premises" — the same job the word "therefore" does in prose. It can't be negated or plugged into a truth table; it sits one level above the formulas, describing the whole argument.

Crucially, everything to the left of ⊢ is required. In P → Q, P ⊢ Q, both P → Q and P have to already be established — bare P ⊢ Q, with no conditional connecting them, would not be valid at all (P and Q could easily be unrelated). The conditional is what supplies the missing link; drop it and the inference collapses.
RulePattern
Modus Ponens (MP)P → Q, P ⊢ Q
Modus Tollens (MT)P → Q, ¬Q ⊢ ¬P
Hypothetical Syllogism (HS)P → Q, Q → R ⊢ P → R
Disjunctive Syllogism (DS)P ∨ Q, ¬P ⊢ Q
Conjunction (Conj)P, Q ⊢ P ∧ Q
Simplification (Simp)P ∧ Q ⊢ P
Addition (Add)P ⊢ P ∨ Q

Verifying the rules mechanically

Building a proof: chaining rules together

A proof is just a sequence of formulas, each one either a premise or licensed by a rule applied to earlier lines. Here's the whole skill, worked out twice.

Example 1 — two applications of Modus Ponens

Given: P → Q, Q → R, P. Prove: R.

1P → Qpremise
2Q → Rpremise
3Ppremise
4Q1, 3, Modus Ponens
5R2, 4, Modus Ponens

Example 2 — chaining Hypothetical Syllogism into Modus Tollens

Given: P → Q, Q → R, ¬R. Prove: ¬P.

1P → Qpremise
2Q → Rpremise
3¬Rpremise
4P → R1, 2, Hypothetical Syllogism
5¬P3, 4, Modus Tollens

Notice neither proof needed a truth table. Example 2 has 3 atomic propositions — a truth table would need 8 rows to prove the same thing. The proof does it in 2 inference steps, and each step is individually checkable.

Proof by contradiction (reductio ad absurdum)

This is the technique behind disproving a claim in a real argument: assume the thing you want to disprove, show it leads to a contradiction, conclude it must be false. Formally: assume A, derive both B and ¬B from it, and since B ∧ ¬B can never be true, the assumption A must be rejected — giving ¬A.

Given: A → B, A → ¬B. Prove: ¬A.

1A → Bpremise
2A → ¬Bpremise
3Aassumption, for reductio
4B1, 3, Modus Ponens
5¬B2, 3, Modus Ponens
6B ∧ ¬B4, 5, Conjunction — contradiction
7¬A3–6, reductio ad absurdum
Why this is the sharpest tool in a debate Whenever someone claims "X is true," you don't have to attack X directly — you can provisionally grant it, follow where it logically leads, and show it collides with something already established or with itself. If it does, X can't be true, full stop. This is the formal version of "let's assume you're right, and see where that goes" — a move you've probably used in conversation without the name.

Spot the invalid look-alike

Two of the most common reasoning errors are one-word rewrites of valid rules. Denying the antecedent looks like Modus Tollens; affirming a disjunct looks like Disjunctive Syllogism. Test both pairs — the invalid one will show a counterexample row.
