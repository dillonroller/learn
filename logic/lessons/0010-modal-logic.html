Modal Logic: Necessity & Possibility

Back in lesson 2, "may," "can," and "must" got flagged as landmines and set aside for later — plain propositional logic has no way to represent them honestly. This is that lesson. Modal logic adds exactly two new symbols on top of everything you already know, and they turn out to follow the same duality pattern you already learned for quantifiers.

Quick recall

From memory:

  • What's the negation of ∀x P(x)?
  • What's the negation of ∃x P(x)?

Answers: ¬∀x P(x) ≡ ∃x ¬P(x) · ¬∃x P(x) ≡ ∀x ¬P(x) — keep this pattern in mind, it's about to reappear.

Two new operators: □ and ◇

Modal logic adds two symbols to everything from lessons 1–9:

SymbolRead asMeaning
□P"necessarily P" / "must be P"P is true no matter how things could have turned out
◇P"possibly P" / "could be P"P is true in at least one way things could have turned out
The quantifier analogy — this is the whole trick □ and ◇ behave exactly like ∀ and ∃, just quantifying over something different. ∀x P(x) quantifies over things in a domain; □P quantifies over ways things could have been — usually called possible worlds. "□P" says P holds in every possible world; "◇P" says P holds in at least one. Same all/some structure, new domain.

A possible world isn't a sci-fi concept — it's just a complete, self-consistent way things could be, including the actual world as one member of the set. "It's raining" is true in the actual world but false in plenty of others (ones where it didn't rain today). "2 + 2 = 4" is true in every possible world — there's no consistent way to imagine it false. That difference is exactly what □ and ◇ are built to express: □(2+2=4) is true; □Raining is false, even on a day when it happens to be raining, because ◇¬Raining is also true — it could easily not have been.

Duality: □ and ◇ mirror ∀ and ∃ exactly

The negation pattern from lesson 6 carries over unchanged:

OriginalEquivalent
¬□P◇¬P
¬◇P□¬P
Read it in English and it's obvious "It's not necessary that P" means exactly "it's possible that not-P" — if P weren't guaranteed, there must be some way things could go where P fails. "It's not possible that P" means exactly "it's necessary that not-P" — if P can't happen anywhere, then not-P holds everywhere. Same shape as lesson 6's quantifier negation: swap "some individual" for "some possible world" and the logic is identical.

Symbolizing "must," "can," and "might"

This is the payoff lesson 2 promised. Recall the ticket example: "you may enter only if you have a ticket" needed Admitted to mean permission, not the physical act — because plain propositional logic had no separate symbol for "may." Now it does.

EnglishModal logic
"You must have a ticket to enter"□(Enter → Ticket)
"You might already know this"◇Know(you, this)
"It can't be raining and not raining"□¬(Rain ∧ ¬Rain)

The modal scope fallacy — where debates actually break

Modal operators interact with → in a way that's extremely easy to slide past unnoticed. Compare these two formulas carefully — they look almost identical, but they claim very different things:

FormulaWhat it actually claims
□(P → Q)The conditional itself is a necessary truth — true in every possible world, whatever P and Q happen to be there
P → □QIf P is actually true, then Q is necessarily true — a much stronger claim about Q specifically
They are not equivalent — a clean example proves it Take P = "It's raining" and Q = "It's raining" (so P and Q are the same proposition). □(P → P) is true — any conditional of the form "if X then X" is a tautology, true no matter what, so it's necessarily true. But P → □P is false on a day when it's actually raining: raining today is a contingent fact, not a necessary one — it easily could have gone the other way. Same letters, same connectives, wildly different scope, wildly different truth value.
Why this matters for evaluating real arguments This exact slide shows up constantly: someone establishes that a conditional is logically guaranteed — "if X, then X follows" is trivially true — and then talks as if that guarantee transfers onto the consequent by itself, as though X were now certain rather than merely conditionally implied. Spotting □(P→Q) quietly turning into P→□Q partway through an argument is one of the sharpest, most underused tools you now have. It's the modal-logic sibling of affirming the consequent — a scope error that "sounds" valid because the individual pieces are each true somewhere.
