Informal Fallacies

Everything through lesson 11 catches broken structure — a truth table or a proof checker doesn't care what P and Q actually mean. Most bad arguments you'll actually hear in the wild aren't like that. Their structure is often fine; the problem is something about the content — an irrelevant attack, a misrepresented position, a chain of claims nobody actually supported. This lesson is the catalog for spotting those.

Quick recall

You've already met three fallacies with broken structure — pure formal fallacies, catchable regardless of subject matter:

  • Affirming the consequent (lesson 2)
  • Denying the antecedent (lesson 5)
  • The modal scope fallacy (lesson 10)

Formal vs. informal fallacies

The dividing line A formal fallacy is a structural error — swap in any P and Q whatsoever and it's still broken, exactly like lesson 3's truth tables showed. An informal fallacy can have perfectly valid-looking structure; the failure is in the content — a premise that's irrelevant, ambiguous, unsupported, or a misrepresentation of what's actually being argued. You can't catch these with a truth table. You have to actually understand what's being claimed.

Fallacies of relevance

Each of these offers a premise that's true, maybe even interesting — but doesn't actually bear on whether the conclusion is true.

Ad Hominem — attacking the arguer, not the argument "You can't trust his argument for raising the speed limit — he got a speeding ticket last year." His driving record has no bearing on whether his premises and logic are correct. Attack the argument, not the person making it.
Careful — not every mention of a person is ad hominem "This safety report was funded entirely by the company whose product it evaluates" is not a fallacy — it's a legitimate reason to weigh a piece of testimony more skeptically (inductive evidence, exactly like lesson 1's inductive/deductive split). The line: attacking someone's character or circumstances to dismiss their argument's logical content is fallacious; flagging a real conflict of interest to calibrate how much weight to give their testimony as evidence is not.
Straw Man — attacking a misrepresented version "My opponent wants to trim the defense budget by 5%. He must want to leave the country defenseless!" A modest, specific proposal gets inflated into an extreme one, the extreme version gets knocked down, and the arguer acts as though they've defeated the original. They haven't — they never engaged with it.
Appeal to (Illegitimate) Authority "This celebrity says this diet works, so it must." Compare: "A peer-reviewed meta-analysis by nutrition researchers found this diet works." Expertise in the relevant field is real, legitimate inductive evidence — celebrity status on an unrelated subject is not. Even legitimate expert testimony is evidence, not proof; it just happens to actually be relevant, unlike fame.
Appeal to Popularity (ad populum) "Millions of people use this app, so it must be the best one." This is exactly the wrong answer flagged all the way back in lesson 1's quiz — popularity was never one of the two things soundness actually requires (valid structure, true premises). How many people believe something has no bearing on whether it's true.

Fallacies of presumption

These smuggle in an assumption the argument needed to earn, but never actually did.

False Dilemma (False Dichotomy) "Either we cut the budget entirely, or the company goes bankrupt." This presents two options as if they're the only ones — ignoring partial cuts, new revenue, restructuring, and everything else in between. It's the rhetorical equivalent of asserting P ∨ Q as if it were forced and exhaustive, when the real situation might have a dozen live options, not two.
Slippery Slope "If we let employees work from home one day a week, soon no one will ever come in, and the company will collapse." Notice the shape: it's chaining conditionals exactly like Hypothetical Syllogism — WFH1Day → WFH More, WFH More → NoOneComesIn, NoOneComesIn → Collapse, therefore WFH1Day → Collapse. HS itself is a perfectly valid rule. The fallacy isn't in the chaining — it's that each individual link is asserted, not actually supported. A slippery slope can be a legitimate argument if every link genuinely holds; it's a fallacy exactly when the links are just assumed.
Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question) "We know the policy is fair because it treats everyone equally, and we know it treats everyone equally because it's fair." The conclusion has been quietly planted as a premise. Recall lesson 5's reductio: a real proof needs premises that are independent of what you're trying to establish. If your premise already assumes the conclusion, you haven't derived anything — you've just restated it in a circle.

Fallacies of weak evidence

Hasty Generalization "I met two rude people from that city, so everyone from there must be rude." In the language of lesson 6, this is illegitimately jumping from Rude(a) ∧ Rude(b) straight to ∀x Rude(x) — a universal claim from a sample size that couldn't possibly support it. This is exactly the inductive-strength question from lesson 1: a strong inductive generalization needs a large, representative sample; two people you happened to meet is neither.
