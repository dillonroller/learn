Argument Mapping & Reconstruction

Every argument in this topic so far has arrived pre-packaged: "Premise 1... Premise 2... Therefore..." Real arguments — spoken in a meeting, buried in an op-ed, thrown out mid-debate — never look like that. They're compressed, reordered, and missing pieces everyone's expected to fill in silently. This lesson is the actual skill of turning that mess back into the clean form lesson 1 started with.

Quick recall

From lesson 1: an argument is one or more premises leading to a conclusion. Everything below is just a disciplined procedure for finding those pieces when nobody hands them to you labeled.

Step 1: find the conclusion first

Counterintuitively, hunt for the conclusion before the premises — it anchors everything else. Ask: what is this person ultimately trying to get me to believe or do? That's the conclusion, no matter where it physically sits in the paragraph.

Conclusion indicator wordsExample
therefore, thus, so, hence"...so we should switch vendors."
this shows / this means / which proves"...which shows the plan won't work."
consequently, it follows that"Consequently, demand will fall."
The conclusion is not always last "We should ban the additive. After all, three independent studies link it to health risks, and the manufacturer has never disputed those findings." The conclusion ("we should ban the additive") is stated first here, with the premises trailing after "after all." Position in the paragraph tells you nothing — only the "what are they trying to convince me of" test does.

Step 2: find the premises

Once you have the conclusion, everything offered in support of it is a premise.

Premise indicator wordsExample
because, since"...because the data doesn't support it."
given that, for the reason that"Given that costs rose 12%..."
after all"After all, no one else does this."

Not every premise gets an indicator word — plenty are just asserted flatly, with the support relationship left implicit. That's normal; you have to infer that a sentence is functioning as a premise from context, not just from vocabulary.

Step 3: surface the unstated premise (enthymemes)

Almost every real argument skips at least one "obvious" step. An argument with a missing premise has a name: an enthymeme. Lesson 1's very first example was already one, compressed:

"Socrates is human, so Socrates is mortal." As stated, this doesn't even mention the connection between humanity and mortality — that link is ∀x(Human(x)→Mortal(x)), "all humans are mortal," and it's completely unstated here. Nobody actually talks in fully spelled-out syllogisms; the missing premise is assumed common ground.
The technique: ask what premise would make it valid Whenever an argument feels like it's missing a step, ask directly: what additional premise, if added, would make the conclusion actually follow? Add the minimal thing that closes the gap. This does one of two things, and both are useful: (1) the missing premise turns out obviously true, and the argument was fine all along, just compressed for efficiency — or (2) the missing premise turns out shaky, unsupported, or false, and that's now your precise target, instead of a vague feeling that "something's off." This is the real antidote to lesson 12's slippery slope: each unjustified "link" in the chain is exactly an implicit premise nobody defended.

Standard form, with implicit premises marked

Once you've got everything, write it out the way lesson 1 always has — numbered, one claim per line — but flag anything you added yourself:

1Socrates is human.stated
2All humans are mortal.implicit
3Therefore, Socrates is mortal.conclusion

Marking a premise "implicit" isn't cheating — it's honesty about what the arguer actually committed to out loud versus what you had to supply to make the reasoning hang together. If they'd object to the implicit premise once it's spelled out, you've found the real disagreement.

Linked vs. convergent premises

Multiple premises can support a conclusion two structurally different ways, and telling them apart matters for how you respond to a rebuttal.

Linked — the premises only work together "All humans are mortal. Socrates is human. Therefore Socrates is mortal." Delete either premise and the argument doesn't just weaken — it completely falls apart. Neither premise supports the conclusion on its own.
Convergent — each premise supports the conclusion independently "This candidate has 20 years of relevant experience. She also has the strongest policy platform. She's also endorsed by leading economists in the field." Each sentence is a separate, self-sufficient reason. Knock one out and the other two still stand, undiminished.
Why the distinction matters in a live debate If premises are linked, one solid rebuttal to a single premise sinks the entire argument. If premises are convergent, a rebuttal to one premise only costs the arguer that one leg — the conclusion may still stand on the others. Knowing which structure you're facing tells you whether a single counterpoint should end the discussion or just narrow it.

Chained arguments: sub-conclusions become premises

Just like lesson 5's multi-step proofs, real arguments often stack: a small argument establishes a sub-conclusion, and that sub-conclusion becomes a premise for the next stage. "Sales dropped every quarter this year (premise) → so the product isn't meeting demand (sub-conclusion) → so the redesign should be prioritized (final conclusion)." Reconstructing arguments like this means mapping each stage separately — the same discipline as checking a proof one line at a time, rather than trying to swallow the whole thing at once.
