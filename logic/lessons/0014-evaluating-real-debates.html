The five-step process

1
Reconstruct. Find the conclusion, list every premise, surface anything implicit, note linked vs. convergent (lesson 13).
2
Check structural validity. Symbolize if it helps. Look for formal fallacies (affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, modal scope). Run the validity test if the structure is precise enough (lessons 1–11).
3
Check for informal fallacies. Relevance, presumption, weak evidence (lesson 12).
4
Assess premise truth — separately. This is empirical, not logical. For each premise, ask: what evidence would actually confirm or deny this?
5
Write the verdict as two separate answers, never blended: is the structure valid? Are the premises well-supported? This is lesson 1's two-question test, now fully operational.

Full walkthrough: an argument nobody cleaned up for you

The argument, as actually spoken "Our office should end remote work entirely. Every major product breakthrough we've ever had happened during in-person brainstorming sessions. If we let people keep working from home, we'll never get another breakthrough like that again, and we'll fall behind our competitors."

Step 1 — Reconstruct

1Every past breakthrough happened during in-person brainstorming.stated
2In-person brainstorming is necessary for any breakthrough.implicit
3If we keep remote work, we'll never brainstorm in person again.stated
4No more breakthroughs → falling behind competitors.stated (unsupported link)
5Therefore, we should end remote work entirely.conclusion

Line 2 is the load-bearing implicit premise — the argument never actually says "in-person is necessary," it only reports a historical pattern (line 1). Without line 2, lines 3–4 don't connect to line 1 at all.

Step 2 — Check structural validity

Symbolize the core move: let B = "a breakthrough occurs," I = "in-person brainstorming happens." Line 2, once made explicit, is □(B → I) — "in-person is necessary for a breakthrough." The argument then reasons: no in-person (¬I) means no breakthrough (¬B) — which is exactly Modus Tollens applied to line 2. Given the implicit premise, the core structure is actually valid. That's an important, easy-to-miss result — this argument is not a formal fallacy. The trouble is entirely somewhere else.

Step 3 — Check for informal fallacies

Hasty Generalization on line 2 "Every breakthrough we've had happened in-person" is one company's limited history — that supports "in-person correlated with our past breakthroughs," not "in-person is necessary for breakthroughs in general." Jumping from a small, single-company sample to a universal necessity claim is exactly lesson 12's hasty generalization.
Slippery Slope on line 4 "No more breakthroughs" sliding into "we'll fall behind our competitors" skips every link in between: are competitors having breakthroughs either? Do breakthroughs alone determine competitiveness? This chain is asserted, not supported — lesson 12's slippery slope, precisely.

Step 4 — Assess premise truth (empirical, not logical)

Logic can't settle this part — but it tells you exactly what to go check. Line 1 needs actual company data (is it really true every breakthrough was in-person, or does that overlook some?). Line 2 needs outside evidence beyond this one company — research on remote vs. in-person ideation, or comparisons with other companies' remote breakthroughs. Line 4 needs competitive analysis, not assertion.

Step 5 — Verdict

Two separate answers, not one blended feeling Structure: valid, given the implicit premise — this is genuine Modus Tollens, not a formal fallacy. Premises: not well-supported — the necessity claim in line 2 is a hasty generalization from a small sample, and line 4 adds an unsupported leap on top. The argument doesn't succeed as given, but not because the reasoning is broken — because two specific, nameable claims need real evidence nobody has offered yet. That's a far more useful conclusion than "I disagree" — it tells the other person exactly what would change your mind.
