Proving Gödel: Arithmetization & the Diagonal Lemma

Lesson 11 gave you the shape of the proof and left the actual machinery as "genuinely intricate — see the primary resource." This lesson is that machinery. By the end you'll be able to build the sentence G yourself instead of taking its existence on faith, and you'll see that the trick isn't really about logic at all — it's the same move as a quine.

The promise, kept literally Lesson 11 said G is "a sentence that, when decoded, says 'G is not provable in this system.'" This lesson answers the question a careful reader should have asked back then: how do you actually build a sentence that refers to itself? Two ingredients do all the work — Gödel numbering and the diagonal lemma.

Ingredient 1 — Gödel numbering: syntax becomes arithmetic

The core move is to assign every symbol, formula, and proof in the system a unique natural number — a Gödel number. One standard scheme: fix a number for each basic symbol (say ¬→1, ∧→2, ∀→3, (→4, variables →5,6,7…), then encode a sequence of symbols s₁s₂s₃…sₙ as

2^s₁ × 3^s₂ × 5^s₃ × 7^s₄ × … × pₙ^sₙ

using successive primes. Because prime factorization is unique, this number can always be decoded back into the exact original symbol sequence — no information is lost. A proof (a sequence of formulas) gets the same treatment one level up: encode the sequence of the formulas' own Gödel numbers.

Why this is the whole ballgame Once every formula φ has a number, write ⌜φ⌝ for "the numeral naming φ's Gödel number." A sentence about numbers can now also be a sentence about formulas and proofs — because a formula's number, a proof's number, and an ordinary number like 7 are all just... numbers. Arithmetic becomes expressive enough to talk about its own syntax. This is the entire reason incompleteness happens to arithmetic and not, say, to a theory of unrelated objects with no way to encode formulas as elements of that theory.

Ingredient 1.5 — representability (the part we'll take as given)

Gödel's real technical labor — the part this lesson won't reproduce, because it's a slog of tedious-but-routine coding, not a conceptual leap — is showing that syntactic relations like "x is the Gödel number of a valid proof of the formula numbered y" are representable: there's an actual formula Proof(x, y) in the language of arithmetic that is true exactly when x really does code a proof of y. This works for the same reason the Church–Turing material in lesson 9 should make plausible: checking a proof is a finite, mechanical, terminating procedure, and every such procedure turns out to be capturable by a formula of arithmetic. Given Proof(x, y), define the provability predicate:

Prov(y)  :=  ∃x. Proof(x, y)

In words: "some x codes a proof of the formula numbered y" — i.e., "y is provable." This is an ordinary arithmetic formula with one free variable, y. Nothing mystical yet — just a very elaborate way of asking a number-theoretic question that happens to mean something about proofs.

Ingredient 2 — the Diagonal Lemma (fixed-point theorem)

The Diagonal Lemma For any formula ψ(x) with one free variable, there exists a sentence φ such that the system proves φ ↔ ψ(⌜φ⌝).

Read that twice — it's saying something wild: for any property ψ you can state, you can build a sentence φ that asserts "ψ is true of me." φ is a fixed point of ψ under the encoding. This is the actual self-reference engine; everything about G falls out of plugging the right ψ in.

The quine analogy — this is the intuition to keep You've written, or at least seen, a quine — a program whose output is its own source code. A quine works by a two-step trick: it contains a piece of data representing "print my own text with the following template," then applies that template to itself. The diagonal lemma's proof is that exact trick, done in arithmetic instead of a programming language. Define a function diag(n): "take the formula numbered n, call it θ(x), and return the Gödel number of θ(⌜θ⌝)" — θ applied to its own code, exactly like a quine's template applied to itself. Now let φ be the formula ψ(diag(x)) evaluated at x = ⌜ψ(diag(x))⌝ — i.e., apply diag to ψ(diag(x))'s own number. Unwinding that substitution mechanically produces exactly φ ↔ ψ(⌜φ⌝). Nothing paradoxical happened; it's arithmetic substitution, done to itself, same as a quine printing itself.

Building G, for real this time

Apply the diagonal lemma to ψ(x) := ¬Prov(x) — "x is not provable." The lemma guarantees a sentence G such that the system proves:

G  ↔  ¬Prov(⌜G⌝)

That's it — that's the sentence lesson 11 described in prose ("G says of itself: I am not provable"), now actually constructed rather than asserted. From here the argument is exactly lesson 11's trap, but now every step is backed by something real instead of an appeal to intuition:

  • Suppose the system proves G. Provability is representable, so the system also proves Prov(⌜G⌝). But it proved G, which is equivalent to ¬Prov(⌜G⌝) — so it proves both Prov(⌜G⌝) and its negation. Contradiction, so a consistent system cannot prove G.
  • Since the system can't prove G, Prov(⌜G⌝) is false — which is exactly what G asserts. So G is true, and unprovable. First Incompleteness Theorem, constructed rather than described.

What this buys you that lesson 11 couldn't

With the machinery in hand, three things that had to be taken on faith before are now checkable claims:

  • Why arithmetic specifically? Because Gödel numbering needs a theory rich enough to encode sequences and do the substitution diag requires — basic arithmetic (Peano-style) is the minimal, natural setting where that's available. A theory too weak to encode its own syntax is simply immune to this argument (and correspondingly too weak to be interesting).
  • Why can't you just "add G as an axiom" and fix it? You can — and the new, stronger system has its own Gödel numbering, its own Prov', and the diagonal lemma builds a new G' for it, unprovable in the new system. The construction is a machine that runs on any system meeting the preconditions, including ones patched to include the previous G.
  • The Second Theorem, sharpened. "The system proves its own consistency" formalizes to ¬Prov(⌜⊥⌝) (the contradiction symbol is never provable). Formalizing the first theorem's argument itself — which requires nothing beyond what the system can already express — shows ¬Prov(⌜⊥⌝) → G is provable. So if the system could prove ¬Prov(⌜⊥⌝), it could prove G, which the first theorem already rules out.

Practice before next lesson

Pick a tiny toy alphabet (say, just {¬, P} with ¬→1, P→2, and use base-10 digit concatenation instead of prime powers to keep the arithmetic light — encode a symbol sequence s₁s₂…sₙ as the number formed by writing the digits in order). Gödel-number the formula ¬P and the formula ¬¬P by hand. Then write, in plain English, what diag would have to do to a formula like ¬P(x) to produce a sentence about "the formula obtained by substituting my own code for x." You don't need to compute the actual number — the goal is to feel the substitution step in your hands once, the way lesson 5 had you feel a proof step by step instead of just reading about proofs.
