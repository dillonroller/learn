Intuitionistic Logic: What Changes Without Excluded Middle

Lesson 5's reductio example proved ¬A by assuming A and deriving a contradiction — and that proof is, quietly, completely fine by the standards of this lesson. What this lesson exposes is a different move classical logic allows without comment: using the exact same contradiction trick to prove a positive claim P, not just a negative one. Intuitionistic logic is what you get when you refuse that second move — and refusing it turns out to change less than you'd expect, and more than you'd expect, in exactly the places that matter.

The law everyone assumes without noticing

Law of Excluded Middle (LEM) P ∨ ¬P — every statement is either true or its negation is. Classically, this is a tautology: check it on a truth table and it comes out true on every row, no matter what P is.

Intuitionistic logic doesn't add "LEM is false" as a rule — it simply doesn't assume it, and doesn't let you derive it for free the way classical logic does. That single omission is the entire difference between the two systems. Everything else in this lesson is downstream of it.

Why omit it? The BHK interpretation of "proof"

Intuitionistic logic reads each connective as a specification for what counts as a proof, not just a truth-table row (the Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation):

  • A proof of P ∧ Q is a pair: a proof of P and a proof of Q.
  • A proof of P ∨ Q is a proof of P with a tag saying which one, or a proof of Q with a tag — you must actually know which disjunct holds, not just that at least one does.
  • A proof of P → Q is a procedure that converts any proof of P into a proof of Q.
  • A proof of ∃x. P(x) is a specific witness a, together with a proof of P(a) — an example you can point to, not an abstract guarantee one exists.
  • ¬P is defined as P → ⊥ — a proof of ¬P is a procedure turning any proof of P into an absurdity.
Now LEM's problem is visible To assert P ∨ ¬P under this reading, you'd need to already have, in hand, either a proof of P or a proof of ¬P. For a statement nobody has resolved yet — an open conjecture, say — you have neither. Classically, LEM asserts it anyway, unconditionally, for every P, resolved or not. Intuitionistically, that's asserting more than you're entitled to: a guarantee that a disjunct is available, made without producing one.

Double negation — the rule that's really doing the work

LEM and double-negation elimination (¬¬P ⊢ P) turn out to be interchangeable — each can be derived from the other using only rules both systems already share. So the practical question is: which familiar proof technique secretly relies on DNE?

Two reductios that look identical and aren't Proving a negation (valid intuitionistically, exactly lesson 5's example): assume P, derive ⊥, conclude ¬P. Under BHK this is just unpacking the definition of ¬P as "P → ⊥" — nothing extra is smuggled in.

Proving an affirmation by contradiction (invalid intuitionistically, valid classically): assume ¬P, derive ⊥, conclude P. This has the exact surface shape of the first move, but it's really an application of double-negation elimination in disguise — you derived ¬¬P (¬P leads to absurdity), then quietly cashed it in for P. BHK asks: where's the actual procedure that builds you a proof of P? A refutation of a refutation of P is not the same object as a proof of P — it doesn't hand you a witness, it just rules out one particular way of denying it.

This is the whole system, in practice: ex falso quodlibet (from ⊥, derive anything — ⊥ ⊢ P) stays valid, because absurdity really does make any procedure vacuously satisfiable. Deriving a negation by contradiction stays valid. What goes away is exactly one move: using a contradiction from ¬P to conclude P outright.

What this costs you, concretely

A short, genuinely surprising list of things that are classically provable but have no intuitionistic proof: P ∨ ¬P itself; ¬¬P → P; and — this is the one that trips people up — ¬(∀x.¬P(x)) → ∃x.P(x) ("not everything fails to satisfy P" does not intuitionistically give you an actual witness where P holds; it only rules out the universal failure, without constructing an example). Meanwhile, the core proof rules from lesson 5 and lesson 8 — Modus Ponens, conjunction/disjunction introduction and elimination, universal instantiation, existential generalization — are entirely unchanged. Intuitionistic logic isn't a different logic built from scratch; it's classical logic's proof system with exactly one exit removed.

Curry–Howard — why this isn't just a philosophical scruple Read P → Q as a function type A → B: a proof of P → Q, under BHK, is a procedure converting a P into a Q — which is exactly what a function of type A → B is. This correspondence (propositions are types, proofs are programs) is a genuine, formal isomorphism, not a loose analogy, called the Curry–Howard correspondence. It's also why this isn't an obscure branch of logic: it's the logic every strongly-typed programming language's type system already implements. A Rust function that type-checks is a constructive proof of the proposition its signature encodes — your compiler has been doing intuitionistic logic the whole time you've been writing Rust.

Practice before next lesson

Take a claim like "there exist irrational numbers a, b such that ab is rational" (a classic example). The classical proof considers √2^√2: either it's rational (done, a=b=√2) or it's irrational (then use it as a, and b=√2, since irrationalirrational = (√2^√2)^√2 = √2^2 = 2, rational) — proved by cases, via LEM, without ever determining which case actually holds. Write out, in your own words, why this proof does not satisfy the BHK reading of ∃ (a specific witness plus a proof it works) — and what would be missing if someone challenged you to actually name the a and b.
