Predicates & quantifiers

TermMeaning
PredicateP(x) — a property; not a proposition until applied to something
RelationP(x,y) — predicate with 2+ slots; argument order matters
Domain of discourseThe set of things being quantified over
∀ (universal)"for all x" — pairs with →
∃ (existential)"there exists an x" — pairs with ∧
The pairing rule ∀x (P(x) → Q(x)) — "everything that's P is also Q." ∃x (P(x) ∧ Q(x)) — "something is both P and Q." Swap the connectives and you get either an absurdly strong claim or a nearly-vacuous one.

Quantifier order

∀x ∃y P(x,y) ≠ ∃y ∀x P(x,y). ∃ before ∀ means one fixed thing works for everyone; ∃ after ∀ allows a different thing per case. Never swap casually.

Proof rules

RulePattern & restriction
Universal Instantiation∀x P(x) ⊢ P(a) — always safe
Existential GeneralizationP(a) ⊢ ∃x P(x) — always safe
Existential Instantiation∃x P(x) ⊢ P(c) — c must be fresh
Universal GeneralizationP(a) ⊢ ∀x P(x) — a must be truly arbitrary

UI and EG only narrow a claim — always safe. UG and EI widen a claim — that's exactly where the restrictions live.
