The Keyboard Map

Before you can compose, you need to know the territory. The keyboard is a map — and once you see its pattern, everything in music theory clicks into place.

The 7 natural notes

Western music names its notes with just 7 letters, then repeats: C D E F G A B, then back to C. That repetition is called an octave.

On the keyboard, these 7 notes are the white keys. The black keys fill the gaps between some of them.

The composer's view These 7 notes are your raw material. Every scale, chord, and melody you'll ever write is built from combinations of them (plus the black keys). Learning their positions is the first act of composition.

Find Middle C — your anchor point

Look at your keyboard. You'll notice the black keys come in groups of 2 and groups of 3, alternating all the way across. This pattern is your landmark.

C is always the white key immediately to the LEFT of any group of 2 black keys.

Find the group of 2 black keys in the middle of your keyboard. The white key just to their left is Middle C — the central reference point for everything.

Click any key to see its name. Highlighted keys are C notes.

The pattern of 7

Once you have Middle C, the other natural notes run in alphabetical order to the right: C → D → E → F → G → A → B → C. Then the pattern repeats.

To the left of Middle C the same notes appear in reverse: B → A → G → F → E → D → C.

Try this now Play Middle C. Then play D, E, F, G, A, B, and the next C above it. You just played a C major scale — the foundation of the most common chords in Western music. It sounds "complete" because your ear has been trained by centuries of music built on this pattern.

The black keys

The 5 black keys in each octave are the sharps and flats — they raise or lower a white note by a half step (the smallest distance on the keyboard).

  • The black key to the right of D is called D♯ (D-sharp)
  • The same key is also called E♭ (E-flat) — same key, two names, depending on context

You don't need to master sharps and flats yet. For now, just notice that there is no black key between E and F, and no black key between B and C. These are the two spots where white keys sit directly next to each other. This asymmetry is why the pattern of 2+3 black keys repeats.

Why this matters for composition

The keyboard's layout isn't arbitrary — it's a physical map of pitch relationships. When you understand this map, you can:

  • Find any note instantly without thinking
  • See visually why some notes sound good together
  • Transpose (shift) a melody to any key just by moving your hand

Every chord progression, every scale, every melody you'll compose has a home on this map. You're learning the geography before you start writing the story.

Practice: Locate the C notes

Your keyboard has several C notes (one per octave). Starting from the lowest note, find every C by looking for the white key left of each group of 2 black keys. Play each one. Notice how they all sound like the same note — just higher or lower.

Then do the same for G (always the white key between the 2nd and 3rd black key in a group of 3).

Check your understanding

1. How many natural (white-key) notes are there before the pattern repeats?

2. To find any C on the keyboard, you look for…

3. Between which two white keys is there NO black key?
