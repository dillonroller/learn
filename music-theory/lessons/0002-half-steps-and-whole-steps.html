Half Steps & Whole Steps

You know the map. Now you need to measure distances on it. Every scale, chord, and melody you'll compose is built from just two distances — the half step and the whole step.

The half step

A half step (also called a semitone) is the smallest distance between any two notes on the keyboard — always the next key, whether white or black.

  • E → F is a half step (white to white, no black key between)
  • B → C is a half step (white to white, no black key between)
  • C → C♯ is a half step (white to the black key directly above)
  • G♯ → A is a half step (black to the white key directly above)
Try it now Place your thumb on Middle C. Now play the very next key to the right — that's C♯, a half step up. Go back and play the very next key to the left — that's B, a half step down. You can't get any smaller than this on the keyboard.

The whole step

A whole step (also called a tone) is simply two half steps stacked together — skip one key, land on the next.

  • C → D is a whole step (skip C♯)
  • D → E is a whole step (skip D♯)
  • A → B is a whole step (skip A♯)
Try it now Play C, then skip C♯ and land on D. That gap you just jumped is a whole step. Play it slowly back and forth — C to D, D to C. This is one of the most important moves in music.

Why this matters for composition

Every scale is just a recipe of half steps and whole steps. The major scale — the one that sounds bright and happy — uses this exact recipe:

W W H W W W H
C→DD→EE→FF→GG→AA→BB→C

That recipe — W W H W W W H — produces the C major scale you played in lesson 1. The same recipe starting on any note produces a major scale in that key. This is how musicians transpose: same recipe, different starting point.

The 12-note chromatic scale

If you play every single key — white and black — from one C up to the next C, you get 12 half steps. This is called the chromatic scale, and it contains all the raw material Western music uses. Every scale, chord, and melody is a selection from these 12 notes.

The composer's insight Scales are filters. The major scale is one filter — it selects 7 of the 12 notes using the W W H W W W H recipe. Minor scales, jazz scales, and pentatonic scales are different filters. Learning to choose the right filter for a mood is one of the core skills of composition.

Practice before next lesson

Sit at your keyboard and try to play the C major scale from memory using the W W H W W W H recipe as a guide. Don't look up which notes — just count steps. If you land on all white keys from C to C, you've got it.
