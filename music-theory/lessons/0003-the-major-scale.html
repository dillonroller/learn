The Major Scale in Any Key

You know the recipe: W W H W W W H. C major is just one application of it. The recipe works from any starting note — and every starting note produces a different key. This is where composition starts to open up.

Quick recall

Before reading on — without looking back — can you answer these from memory?

  • How many half steps are in a whole step?
  • What is the major scale recipe?
  • Between which two pairs of white keys is there no black key?

Answers: 2 · W W H W W W H · E–F and B–C

Building G major

Start on G. Apply the recipe step by step:

G
W→
A
W→
B
H→
C
W→
D
W→
E
W→
F♯
H→
G

Notice F♯ — not F. When you apply the recipe honestly from G, the 7th step forces you onto a black key. This is not an exception; it's the recipe doing its job. G major has one sharp: F♯.

Try it now Find G on your keyboard (the white key between the 2nd and 3rd black key in any group of 3). Play G A B C D E F♯ G. It should sound exactly like the major scale — same feel as C major, just higher.

Building F major

Start on F. Apply the recipe:

F
W→
G
W→
A
H→
B♭
W→
C
W→
D
W→
E
H→
F

This time the recipe lands on B♭ (B-flat) — the black key just to the left of B. F major has one flat: B♭.

Try it now Play F G A B♭ C D E F. The B♭ is the black key between A and B. If it sounds like the same "happy" pattern as C and G major, you've got it.

What is a "key"?

A key is just the set of 7 notes your major scale gives you. When a piece is "in G major", it mostly uses the notes G A B C D E F♯ — that's its palette.

As a composer, choosing a key is choosing your palette before you paint. You're not locked in — music drifts in and out of keys all the time — but the key gives you a home base, a set of notes that all sound good together.

The composer's insight Every chord you'll ever use is built from the notes of a scale. Once you know your key, you know which chords belong to it. That's lesson 4. For now, just feel how each key has its own character — G major has a slight brightness from F♯; F major has a slight warmth from B♭.
