Deontic Logic — Obligation & Permission

This lesson exists because of a single word: should. Back in Logic lesson 13, the example argument was "We should ban the additive — three studies link it to health risks." That "should" is doing something no tool from the Logic curriculum can formally handle. This lesson gives it one.

The is-ought gap

Every formula you built in Logic — P, Rude(a), ∀x Trust(x) — describes what is the case. "Should," "ought," "obligatory," "permissible" describe something different: what ought to be the case. David Hume pointed out in 1739 that you cannot validly get from pure "is" premises to an "ought" conclusion — the two are different kinds of claim, and no amount of stacking descriptive facts logically forces a prescriptive one.

Look at the additive argument again P1: Three studies link the additive to health risks. (is)
P2: The manufacturer hasn't disputed this. (is)
C: We should ban it. (ought)
As written, this is missing an implicit premise — something like "anything shown to pose health risks ought to be banned." Without a normative premise somewhere, no normative conclusion can validly follow. This is Hume's is-ought gap, and it's not a quirk of this one example — it's a hole in every argument that ends in a "should" unless a normative premise bridges it.

Reusing modal logic, with a different reading

Recall Logic lesson 10: □p meant "p is true in every possible world," and ◇p meant "p is true in at least one possible world." Deontic logic doesn't invent new machinery — it takes the exact same box/diamond structure and narrows the worlds under consideration to deontically ideal worlds: the worlds where everyone does what they ought to do.

O — Obligatory Op ("it is obligatory that p") means p holds in every deontically ideal world — every way things ought to go. Compare directly to □p: same shape, normative reading instead of alethic (necessity) reading.
P — Permissible Pp ("it is permissible that p") means p holds in at least one deontically ideal world — some way things could go while everyone still does their duty. Compare to ◇p.
F — Forbidden Fp ("it is forbidden that p") is defined as O¬p — obligatory that p not happen. If lying is forbidden, then not-lying is obligatory in every ideal world.

Duality — the same pattern as lesson 10

Logic lesson 10 showed ◇p ≡ ¬□¬p ("possible" = "not necessarily not"). Deontic logic has the exact same duality: Pp ≡ ¬O¬p — "permissible" means "not obligatory that it doesn't happen." If you're not required to refrain from something, you're permitted to do it. And symmetrically: Op ≡ ¬P¬p — if p is obligatory, then not-p is not permissible.

One axiom that has no alethic parallel Standard deontic logic adds Op → Pp: whatever is obligatory is also permissible. This rules out anything being simultaneously obligatory and forbidden — a system can't require you to do the very thing it forbids. Alethic modal logic has no equivalent axiom, because "necessary" and "impossible" aren't in the same kind of tension "obligatory" and "forbidden" are — this axiom is specifically about consistent duty, not consistent fact.

A paradox worth knowing about

Deontic logic isn't fully settled — the field has a famous list of puzzles where the formalism produces conclusions that sound wrong. The best-known is the Good Samaritan Paradox: "It ought to be that Jones helps the man he robbed" seems to logically entail "It ought to be that Jones robbed the man" (you can't help someone you didn't rob), which is absurd — nothing ought to involve Jones robbing anyone. The paradox arises from how naturally standard deontic logic lets obligations "leak" through logical entailment. No single fix is universally accepted; it's flagged here so you know the field has open problems, the same honest caveat Logic lesson 9 gave about formal systems generally.
