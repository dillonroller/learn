Epistemic Logic — Knowledge & Belief

Last lesson took modal logic's □/◇ and reread them normatively, as obligation and permission. This lesson takes the same move in a different direction: rereading them as knowledge and belief. "I know that p" and "I believe that p" sound similar in English but behave completely differently once you formalize them — and the gap between them is exactly what makes knowledge worth having.

K and B — reusing the pattern again

Same trick as deontic logic: instead of "true in every possible world" or "true in every ideal world," epistemic logic restricts to worlds consistent with what the agent knows — every way things could be, given everything they currently know.

K — Knowledge Kp ("the agent knows that p") means p holds in every world consistent with their knowledge. If there's even one world consistent with what they know where p is false, they don't know p — they might believe it, hope it, or guess it, but not know it.
B — Belief Bp ("the agent believes that p") means p holds in every world consistent with what they believe. Structurally identical to K — but belief-worlds and knowledge-worlds are not the same set, which is exactly where the two operators pull apart.

Factivity — the axiom that separates knowledge from belief

Kp → p — Knowledge is factive If you know p, then p is actually true. You cannot know something false — "I knew it would rain, but it didn't" is a contradiction in terms; what you actually had was a false belief. Crucially, belief has no such axiom: Bp → p is not valid. You can believe all kinds of false things. This single axiom is the entire formal difference between the two operators — everything else about their structure is identical.

This connects directly to the classical analysis of knowledge as justified true belief (JTB): true is doing the work of the factivity axiom above, and justified is what's supposed to rule out lucky-guess true beliefs from counting as knowledge. It doesn't fully succeed — Edmund Gettier's famous 1963 counterexamples show cases of justified true belief that still don't feel like knowledge — but that's a deeper rabbit hole for the dedicated Epistemology lesson later in this topic. For now: factivity is the one formal property knowledge has that belief doesn't.

Do you know what you know?

Two further axioms show up often in epistemic logic, and both are more controversial than factivity:

Positive introspection — Kp → KKp If you know p, you know that you know it. Sounds obvious, but it's a strong claim — it says your access to your own knowledge is perfect, with no room for cases where you actually know something but aren't sure you do.
Negative introspection — ¬Kp → K¬Kp If you don't know p, you know that you don't know it. Even stronger, and more contested — real people are frequently unaware of their own ignorance (they neither know p, nor know that they fail to know it).

Whether to include these axioms is exactly the kind of open modeling choice Logic lesson 9 flagged for formal systems generally: different epistemic logics (S4 includes positive introspection, S5 includes both) make different bets about how self-aware an idealized reasoner is, and the "right" choice depends on what you're modeling.

Duality, one more time

Same pattern as lessons 9 and 10: ¬K¬p reads as "not known to be false" — call it "epistemically possible." Just like ◇p ≡ ¬□¬p and Pp ≡ ¬O¬p, an epistemically possible claim is simply one your knowledge doesn't rule out — which is a much weaker thing than a claim you actually know to be true.
