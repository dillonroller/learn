# Mission: Probability

## Why
Split off from [[../rationality/MISSION.md|Rationality]], which deliberately kept probability light-touch — just enough Bayes/base-rate math for everyday judgment calls, explicitly ruling out "full formal decision theory / utility theory / game theory." The user wants a lot more than that: real probability theory for its own sake, with the same rigor and proof-driven style they responded to in [[../logic/MISSION.md|Logic]]'s mastery phase (Gödel's actual proof, not just the concept). This is the place for that depth — distributions, expectation, variance, and eventually decision theory and maybe game theory — without diluting Rationality's practical, relationship-focused scope.

## Success looks like
- Define a probability space rigorously (sample space, events, axioms) and explain why the axioms are the ones they are
- Work fluently with random variables and their distributions (discrete and continuous), not just single-event probabilities
- Compute and interpret expectation and variance, and explain what they do and don't tell you about a distribution
- Reach the point where the [[../rationality/lessons/0004-calibration-and-expected-value.html|Rationality]] topic's expected-value lesson reads as a special case of something much more general, not the whole story

## Constraints
- No formal probability/statistics background, but very strong technical/CS background (comfortable with proofs, formal notation, discrete math) — can move fast through anything that's "just notation" and spend the saved time on the parts that are genuinely subtle
- Self-study with an AI teacher
- Keep examples secular/neutral (same constraint carried over from [[../logic/NOTES.md|Logic]] and [[../rationality/NOTES.md|Rationality]]) — dice, cards, coins, real datasets, not politically or religiously charged examples

## Out of scope
- Measure-theoretic probability (σ-algebras, Lebesgue integration) — build real intuition and rigor at the level of a strong undergraduate course, not a graduate one
- A full statistics curriculum (hypothesis testing, regression, experimental design) — likely a separate future topic if it comes up
- Game theory as its own multi-lesson arc — a light mention when relevant, not the mission's focus (may become its own topic later)
