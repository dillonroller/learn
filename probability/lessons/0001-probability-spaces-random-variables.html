Probability Spaces & Random Variables

Rationality had you compute P(H|E) dozens of times without ever pinning down what "P" formally means. That was deliberate — Rationality's job was the habit of updating on evidence, not the foundations underneath it. This topic is the foundations. By the end of this lesson you'll know exactly what a probability actually is, and what a "random variable" is — which, despite the name, is neither random nor a variable.

The sample space — every possible outcome, named

Start with Ω (omega), the sample space: the set of every possible outcome of some experiment. Roll one die: Ω = {1,2,3,4,5,6}. Flip a coin twice: Ω = {HH, HT, TH, TT}. An event is just any subset of Ω — "the die shows even" is the event {2,4,6}. Nothing controversial yet; this is bookkeeping. The interesting part is what function turns a set of outcomes into a probability.

The axioms — three rules that generate everything else

The Kolmogorov Axioms A probability function P, assigning a number to every event, must satisfy:
  • Non-negativity: P(A) ≥ 0 for every event A
  • Normalization: P(Ω) = 1 — something in the sample space always happens
  • Countable additivity: if A and B can't both happen (disjoint), P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)

That's the entire foundation — three axioms, and everything else in probability is a theorem derived from them. A couple of derivations worth doing once by hand, because they show the axioms actually earning their keep rather than just being asserted: since Ω and ∅ are disjoint and their union is Ω, additivity plus normalization forces P(∅) = 0. Since A and "not A" are disjoint and union to Ω, additivity forces P(not A) = 1 − P(A) — the complement rule you've been using without proof since Rationality's disease-test example is really just this theorem, applied silently.

Random variables — a function, not a "variable"

The definition that surprises people A random variable X is a function from the sample space to the real numbers: X : Ω → ℝ. That's it — a deterministic, ordinary function. Roll two dice; let X be their sum. X isn't "random" in the sense of being undetermined — for any specific outcome ω (say, (3,5)), X(ω) is a fixed number (8), every time. The randomness lives entirely in which ω actually occurs, not in the function X itself. "Random variable" is a genuinely misleading name for what is, mathematically, the least random object in the whole setup.

Why bother with this indirection instead of just talking about outcomes directly? Because X lets you ask questions about a number derived from an outcome ("what's the sum?") without caring which specific outcome produced it — (3,5), (4,4), and (5,3) are different outcomes but the same value of X. This is the move that makes probability tractable for anything more complex than listing every outcome by hand.

The distribution — X's own probability rules

Once you have a random variable X, its distribution is the assignment of probabilities to X's possible values — derived from P on Ω, but usable on its own. For discrete X (like a die roll or a dice sum), this is the probability mass function (PMF): p(x) = P(X = x). Sum two fair dice: p(7) = 6/36 (six of the 36 equally likely outcomes sum to 7), the most likely single value, while p(2) = p(12) = 1/36, the least likely. Continuous random variables (like a measured height, or time until an event) need a different tool — a probability density rather than a mass — which this topic will build properly in a later lesson rather than rushing here.

Practice before next lesson

Define the full probability space for rolling two dice and looking at their sum: write out Ω (all 36 outcome pairs, or at least describe it precisely), pick one event (e.g. "sum is at least 10") and list exactly which outcomes belong to it, then state the random variable X in words and compute p(x) for two or three values of your choosing. The goal is to feel the difference between Ω (36 outcomes), an event (a subset), and X (a function reducing each outcome to one number) in your own hands.
