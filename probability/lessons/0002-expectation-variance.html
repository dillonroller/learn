Expectation & Variance

Rationality's expected value lesson gave you EV = Σ p(outcome) × value(outcome) as a practical formula. That formula has a proper name — expectation — and now that lesson 1 gave you random variables to plug into it, it comes with a genuinely surprising superpower: you can compute it without ever knowing the full distribution.

Expectation, formally

Expectation For a discrete random variable X with possible values x and PMF p(x): E[X] = Σ x · p(x).

This is exactly Rationality's EV formula, just written with X's own values instead of generic "outcomes" — value(outcome) was always secretly a random variable, and p(outcome) was always secretly its distribution. Summing two dice (from lesson 1): E[X] = 2(1/36) + 3(2/36) + ... + 12(1/36) = 7. Exactly the middle of the possible range — no surprise for a symmetric distribution, but the next section shows a much faster way to have gotten that same 7.

Linearity of expectation — the shortcut that shouldn't work this well

Linearity of Expectation E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y] — always. Not just when X and Y are independent. Not just when they're uncorrelated. Always, unconditionally, for any two random variables on the same probability space.

This deserves to feel surprising. Computing the sum of two dice's expectation directly (as above) required knowing the full 11-value distribution of the sum — real work, built from all 36 outcome pairs. Linearity skips all of it: let X₁ be die 1, X₂ be die 2. E[X₁] = E[X₂] = 3.5 each (average of 1 through 6). So E[X₁+X₂] = 3.5 + 3.5 = 7 — the same answer, computed from two one-line calculations instead of building the whole joint distribution.

Where this actually pays off The shortcut becomes indispensable once "dependent" enters the picture. Classic example: shuffle a deck of 52 cards; how many cards, on average, end up in their own original position? Let Xᵢ = 1 if card i is in position i, else 0 — these Xᵢ are wildly dependent on each other (if 51 cards are fixed, the 52nd is forced too). Computing the distribution of the total directly is a real combinatorics problem. Linearity sidesteps it entirely: E[total] = E[X₁] + E[X₂] + ... + E[X₅₂], and each E[Xᵢ] = P(card i is fixed) = 1/52 by symmetry — so the answer is 52 × (1/52) = 1, on average exactly one card lands in its own spot, regardless of dependence. No joint distribution required, ever.

Variance — how spread out, not just where centered

Two random variables can have the identical expectation and still be nothing alike. A fair coin paying $0 or $2 has E = $1. A trick coin paying exactly $1 always also has E = $1. Expectation alone can't tell them apart — you need a second number for spread.

Variance & Standard Deviation Var(X) = E[(X − E[X])²] — the average squared distance from the mean. Equivalently (often easier to compute): Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])². Standard deviation is σ = √Var(X) — back in the original units, since variance's squaring distorts the scale.

For the $0-or-$2 coin: Var = 0.5(0−1)² + 0.5(2−1)² = 0.5 + 0.5 = 1, so σ = 1. For the guaranteed $1: Var = 0 — no spread at all, since it never deviates from its own mean. Same expectation, completely different risk profile — which is precisely the gap Rationality's expected-utility correction gestured at without the formal tool to measure it: two bets with equal EV are not equally comfortable to take, and variance is the number that says by how much.

Check your understanding

1. Why does linearity of expectation hold even when X and Y are dependent?

2. In the card-shuffle example, why is linearity so much easier than computing the distribution directly?

3. Two bets both have E[payout] = $100. Bet A always pays exactly $100. Bet B pays $0 or $200 with equal probability. What distinguishes them?

4. Why take the square root of variance to get standard deviation, rather than just using variance directly?

Practice before next lesson

Compute, by hand, E[X] and Var(X) for a single fair six-sided die (X = the value rolled, each value 1–6 with probability 1/6). Then use linearity to find E[X₁+X₂+X₃] for three dice rolled together, without computing the sum's full distribution. Finally, invent two different bets with the same expectation as each other but visibly different variance, the way this lesson's $100 example did — the goal is to feel expectation and variance as two genuinely separate pieces of information, not one number in disguise.
