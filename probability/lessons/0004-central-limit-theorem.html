The Central Limit Theorem

Lesson 3 introduced the Normal distribution as one option among several. This lesson explains why it isn't just "one option" in practice — it's the distribution that sums and averages converge to almost no matter what you started with. That's a genuinely strange, powerful claim, and unlike Gödel's proof, this lesson builds the actual mechanism, not just the statement.

The statement

Central Limit Theorem Let X₁, X₂, ..., Xₙ be independent, identically distributed random variables with finite mean μ and variance σ² — any distribution with those two finite numbers, discrete or continuous, symmetric or wildly skewed. Let Sₙ = X₁+...+Xₙ. Then as n → ∞, the standardized sum (Sₙ − nμ)/(σ√n) converges in distribution to the standard Normal(0,1) — regardless of what distribution the Xᵢ actually came from.

Read the "regardless" literally: the Xᵢ could be Bernoulli coin flips, Poisson counts, or a distribution so lopsided it barely resembles a bell curve at all — the theorem doesn't care. Only two numbers about the original distribution matter (μ and σ²); everything else about its shape gets washed out by the act of summing many of them.

Why the sample mean's spread shrinks like 1/n

The sample mean X̄ₙ = Sₙ/n is what you actually compute in practice (an average, not a raw sum). Using linearity of expectation: E[X̄ₙ] = E[Sₙ]/n = nμ/n = μ — the sample mean is centered exactly on the true mean, for any n. Using lesson 3's variance-additivity under independence: Var(Sₙ) = n·σ² (the Xᵢ are independent by assumption), so Var(X̄ₙ) = Var(Sₙ)/n² = σ²/n. The sample mean's spread shrinks as n grows — averaging over more data genuinely does make your estimate more concentrated around the truth, and this is the precise rate at which it happens.

Why it converges to Normal, specifically — a real proof sketch

The mechanism uses a tool worth having on hand generally: the moment generating function M_X(t) = E[e^(tX)]. Two facts about MGFs make the proof work: MGFs of independent sums multiply (M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t)·M_Y(t)), and — stated without proof here, a real but separate theorem — a distribution's MGF, where it exists, determines that distribution uniquely.

The derivation Let Y = (X−μ)/σ (a standardized single Xᵢ, so E[Y]=0, E[Y²]=1). The standardized sum is Zₙ = ΣYᵢ/√n. By independence, MGFs multiply: M_{Zₙ}(t) = [M_Y(t/√n)]ⁿ. Near 0, any well-behaved MGF has the Taylor expansion M_Y(s) = 1 + s·E[Y] + (s²/2)·E[Y²] + o(s²) = 1 + s²/2 + o(s²) (using E[Y]=0, E[Y²]=1). Substituting s = t/√n: 
M_{Zₙ}(t) = [1 + t²/(2n) + o(1/n)]ⁿ  →  e^(t²/2)   as n → ∞
using the standard limit (1 + x/n)ⁿ → eˣ. And e^(t²/2) is exactly the MGF of a standard Normal(0,1) — which, by the uniqueness fact above, means Zₙ's distribution itself converges to standard Normal. The entire shape of the original distribution disappeared into the o(s²) term, which vanishes in the limit — only μ and σ² survived to matter.

What the theorem does not say

"CLT means my raw data becomes normally distributed with enough samples" — No. The theorem is about the distribution of the sum or average of many draws, not about the individual data points themselves. Roll a fair die a million times: each individual roll is still exactly Uniform{1,...,6}, flat, forever — rolling more dice doesn't reshape a single die's own distribution. What becomes approximately Normal is the average of many rolls, a completely different random variable from any single roll.
"CLT works no matter what, for any n" — The theorem is a statement about a limit as n → ∞; it doesn't promise how large n needs to be for the approximation to already be good, and that answer genuinely depends on the original distribution (a very skewed starting distribution needs a larger n before the Normal approximation looks convincing than a starting distribution that's already close to symmetric).

Practice before next lesson

Take the flattest, least "normal-looking" distribution you can think of — say, a fair die (Uniform{1,...,6}), or even a two-valued Bernoulli(0.5). Imagine (or actually simulate, if you want to write a few lines of code) averaging 2 rolls, then 5, then 30. Describe, in your own words, how you'd expect the shape of "average of k rolls" to change as k grows — from flat/two-spike toward bell-shaped — and connect that directly to the μ, σ²/k prediction from this lesson rather than just eyeballing it.
