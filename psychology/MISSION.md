# Mission: Psychology

## Why
To understand *why* human reasoning systematically diverges from the formal logic and practical rationality already learned — not just to catalog more biases, but to see the actual cognitive-science research (dual-process theory, the psychology-of-reasoning experimental tradition) that explains the gap between how people *can* reason and how they *actually* do. This topic exists because the user asked, mid-conversation, whether psychology is related to logic — the honest answer opened up a whole empirical research field worth exploring for its own sake.

## Success looks like
- Explain System 1 / System 2 (dual-process theory) and predict, for a given reasoning task, which system is likely driving an answer
- Work through the Wason selection task from memory and explain why the abstract and social-contract versions produce wildly different success rates
- Recognize belief bias — judging an argument's validity by how plausible its conclusion sounds rather than its logical structure — in a live example
- Explain, for a given informal fallacy from [[../logic/MISSION.md|Logic]] or bias from [[../rationality/MISSION.md|Rationality]], what psychological research says is actually happening cognitively when it occurs

## Constraints
- No prior formal psychology background, but has completed the full 14-lesson Logic curriculum and the 3-lesson Rationality curriculum — comfortable with formal notation, already knows validity/soundness and the major cognitive biases from a practical-use angle
- Self-study with an AI teacher

## Out of scope
- Re-teaching the cognitive biases already covered practically in [[../rationality/MISSION.md|Rationality]] (confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, fundamental attribution error, availability heuristic) — this topic references those rather than repeating them, and focuses on the research/theory layer underneath
- Clinical psychology, therapy, mental health / disorders — this topic is about cognition and reasoning, not clinical practice
- Developmental, social, and personality psychology broadly — scoped tightly to the psychology of reasoning and judgment for now; can expand later if the user's interest broadens
