# Mission: Quantum Mechanics

## Why
After finishing the core [[../logic/MISSION.md|Logic]] and [[../rationality/MISSION.md|Rationality]] curricula and going deeper into Logic's mastery phase, the user asked to learn "something else about reality" — modern, rigorous, and not the kind of thing most people actually understand rather than just recognize as a buzzword. Quantum mechanics was their own first example. The explicit goal is the real mathematical formalism (state vectors, superposition, entanglement, the measurement postulate) and the genuine interpretation debate — not pop-science metaphors ("it's a wave and a particle," "the cat is alive and dead"), which the user already has and finds unsatisfying.

## Success looks like
- Represent a quantum state as a vector in a complex vector space and explain what superposition actually means mathematically (not "both at once," but a linear combination with amplitudes)
- Apply the Born rule to compute measurement probabilities from a state vector
- Explain entanglement precisely enough to say why it's not the same as classical correlation or hidden information
- Describe at least two rival interpretations (e.g. Copenhagen, many-worlds) accurately enough to state what each one actually claims and where they disagree — without picking a winner, since this is a genuinely open question
- Recognize when a popular claim about quantum mechanics ("proves consciousness affects reality," "explains free will") is using the real physics vs. borrowing its vocabulary for something the physics doesn't say — the same rhetorical-discipline instinct [[../logic/lessons/0011-godels-incompleteness-theorems.html#incompleteness-misuse|Logic lesson 11]] built for Gödel

## Constraints
- No prior physics background beyond general familiarity, but very strong technical/CS background (linear algebra, complex numbers, and formal notation should all be comfortable or fast to pick up) — a linear-algebra-first, computer-science-flavored approach (à la quantum computing courses) is likely a better fit than a physics-textbook approach starting from classical mechanics and wave equations
- Self-study with an AI teacher
- Keep the philosophical-implications material honest about what's established physics vs. open interpretation vs. outright misuse — same rigor-over-rhetoric standard as Logic

## Out of scope
- The full mathematical machinery of quantum field theory or the Standard Model — this mission is non-relativistic quantum mechanics
- Quantum computing as an application (circuits, algorithms like Shor's/Grover's) — may be a natural future topic, but the mission here is understanding quantum mechanics itself first
- Picking a "correct" interpretation — presenting the live debate accurately is success; resolving it is not the goal (and isn't resolved by anyone)
