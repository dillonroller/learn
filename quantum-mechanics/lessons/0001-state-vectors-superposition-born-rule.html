State Vectors, Superposition & the Born Rule

Every pop-science account of quantum mechanics eventually says something like "the particle is in two places at once." That sentence is standing in for a specific piece of math. This lesson gives you that math directly — vectors, not vagueness — so you can see exactly what "superposition" means, and exactly where the popular gloss starts to mislead.

A quantum state is a vector

Take the simplest possible quantum system: a qubit — anything with exactly two distinguishable outcomes (a photon's polarization, an electron's spin). Its state lives in a 2-dimensional complex vector space, with two special basis vectors written in "ket" notation:

|0⟩ = (1, 0)      |1⟩ = (0, 1)

Any valid state of the qubit is some vector built from these two:

|ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩       where α, β are complex numbers

If you've done linear algebra, this is nothing new — it's just a vector written in a basis. The physics is entirely in what α and β are allowed to mean, which the next two sections build up precisely.

Superposition — a precise claim, not a vague one

What superposition actually says |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ is one single, perfectly well-defined vector — not "secretly two states happening at once," and not "we don't know which one it's in yet." It's a determinate mathematical object, in exactly the same sense that the vector (3, 4) is one determinate point in the plane, not "secretly both (3,0) and (0,4) happening simultaneously." What makes it feel strange is only revealed in the next section: what happens when you measure it.

One constraint on α and β: the state must be normalized, |α|² + |β|² = 1. This isn't an extra physics assumption bolted on — it's what makes the next section's probabilities sum to 1, the same normalization axiom from ordinary probability theory. Quantum mechanics doesn't get to skip Kolmogorov's axioms; it satisfies them via a specific, unusual rule for generating the probabilities in the first place.

The Born rule — from vector to probability

The Born Rule Measuring |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ in the {|0⟩, |1⟩} basis gives outcome 0 with probability |α|², and outcome 1 with probability |β|². After the measurement, the state is no longer |ψ⟩ — it has become exactly |0⟩ or exactly |1⟩, whichever was observed.

This is the actual content behind "the particle is in two places until observed." Before measurement, the state genuinely is the single vector α|0⟩ + β|1⟩ — not one or the other, and not "both" in any everyday sense either; it's its own kind of mathematical object with no classical analogue. The Born rule tells you the odds of what you'll see if you measure it. After you measure, the superposition is gone — the state has collapsed to whichever basis vector matched the outcome. This transition — irreversible, and not obviously derivable from the rest of quantum mechanics' otherwise perfectly reversible equations — is the seed of what's called the measurement problem, and it's exactly where the rival interpretations (Copenhagen, many-worlds, and others) start to disagree, which a later lesson takes on directly.

Worked example |ψ⟩ = (1/√2)|0⟩ + (1/√2)|1⟩. Check normalization: |1/√2|² + |1/√2|² = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1 ✓. The Born rule gives P(0) = 1/2 and P(1) = 1/2 — a fair coin, encoded as a specific vector rather than as an unknown classical fact waiting to be revealed. The difference from an actual coin flip: a hidden classical coin already landed heads or tails before you looked, you just didn't know which. This qubit, before measurement, is not "one of the two, secretly" — the standard formalism treats it as genuinely, determinately in the superposition state, full stop, until measurement forces a definite outcome.

Before citing "superposition" outside physics

Same discipline as Logic lesson 11's Gödel-misuse check: whenever "quantum superposition" gets invoked outside an actual physical system with a defined state vector and basis — to explain consciousness, free will, or "quantum healing" — ask precisely what the state vector is, what basis it's being measured in, and what the Born rule predicts. If there's no answer, the term is borrowing quantum mechanics' vocabulary and mystique without doing any of quantum mechanics' actual work.

Practice before next lesson

Take the state |ψ⟩ = (√3/2)|0⟩ + (1/2)|1⟩. Check it's normalized, then compute P(0) and P(1) under the Born rule by hand. Then invent your own normalized two-outcome state (pick any α, β with |α|²+|β|²=1) and compute its measurement probabilities too — the goal is to do the arithmetic yourself at least twice before the next lesson introduces entanglement, which needs this comfort with single-qubit states as a prerequisite.
