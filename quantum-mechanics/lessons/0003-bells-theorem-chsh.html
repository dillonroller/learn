Bell's Theorem & the CHSH Inequality

Lesson 2 left the EPR worry unresolved: maybe entangled qubits just carry predetermined, hidden values, and the "spooky" correlation is nothing more than two matched gloves in sealed boxes. This lesson builds the actual inequality that any such hidden-value account must obey — and shows that quantum mechanics predicts a correlation that violates it. This isn't philosophy anymore; it's an algebraic proof plus an experiment.

The setup: two settings each, not one

Alice and Bob share an entangled pair. Each independently picks one of two possible measurement settings — Alice picks a₁ or a₂, Bob picks b₁ or b₂ (think: measuring spin along different axes) — and each gets an outcome of +1 or −1. Crucially, Alice doesn't know which setting Bob chose, and vice versa — this is what "local" will mean below. Define the correlation for a given pair of settings as E(a,b) = average of (Alice's outcome) × (Bob's outcome), over many repeated trials.

Formalizing "local hidden variables"

The local hidden-variable (LHV) assumption Each pair carries a hidden variable λ, drawn from some distribution, fixed at creation. Alice's outcome for setting a is some function A(a, λ) ∈ {+1,−1}, and Bob's outcome for setting b is some function B(b, λ) ∈ {+1,−1} — critically, A depends only on a and λ, never on Bob's setting b (locality), and vice versa. This is exactly the EPR picture: the "instructions" for every possible measurement were fixed in advance, independent of what the other side does.

The CHSH inequality — an algebraic fact about any LHV theory

CHSH Inequality (Clauser–Horne–Shimony–Holt, 1969) Define S = E(a₁,b₁) + E(a₁,b₂) + E(a₂,b₁) − E(a₂,b₂). Under any local hidden-variable theory: |S| ≤ 2.

This isn't a physical assumption — it's pure algebra, true for any LHV assignment whatsoever. For a fixed λ, look at the bracketed quantity A(a₁,λ)[B(b₁,λ)+B(b₂,λ)] + A(a₂,λ)[B(b₁,λ)−B(b₂,λ)]. Since B(b₁,λ) and B(b₂,λ) are each ±1, exactly one of two things happens: either they're equal, making [B(b₁,λ)+B(b₂,λ)] = ±2 and [B(b₁,λ)−B(b₂,λ)] = 0; or they're opposite, making the first bracket 0 and the second ±2. Either way, the whole expression evaluates to exactly ±2 — never more, since A(a,λ) ∈ {+1,−1} just flips the sign. Averaging ±2 (a bounded quantity) over any distribution of λ can never exceed 2 in absolute value: |S| ≤ 2. No physics has been assumed anywhere in this argument — only that outcomes are real numbers fixed in {+1,−1} in advance, independent of the other side's choice.

What quantum mechanics actually predicts

For the entangled Bell state from lesson 2, measured along axes separated by angle θ, the Born rule gives E(a,b) = −cos(θ_a − θ_b). Choosing the angles a₁=0°, a₂=45°, b₁=22.5°, b₂=67.5° (each adjacent pair 22.5° apart):

S = −cos(22.5°) − cos(67.5°) − cos(22.5°) − (−cos(112.5°))
  = 2√2  ≈  2.828
2.828 > 2 — the LHV bound, violated Quantum mechanics predicts a correlation strictly larger than any local hidden-variable theory can ever produce, for any choice of λ-distribution and any functions A, B whatsoever. This specific maximum (2√2) is called Tsirelson's bound — the actual ceiling quantum mechanics imposes, itself an interesting further fact (nature doesn't violate CHSH maximally; it violates it up to exactly this amount and no further). Real experiments — starting with Alain Aspect's in 1982, and "loophole-free" versions closing every known experimental gap by 2015 — have measured S values matching the quantum prediction, not the classical bound. Local hidden variables, as formalized above, are experimentally ruled out.
What this does and doesn't settle Bell's theorem rules out theories that are simultaneously local (no influence faster than light) and realist (definite values exist before measurement) in the specific form formalized above. It does not, by itself, tell you which assumption to give up — different interpretations make different choices. Many-worlds keeps realism (the full wavefunction is always real and determinate) but denies that measurement picks out one single outcome at all. Standard (Copenhagen-style) quantum mechanics denies predetermined values exist before measurement in the first place. Bohmian mechanics keeps hidden variables but gives up locality outright. All three remain live, debated positions — this is exactly the kind of open question this topic's mission commits to presenting accurately rather than resolving.

Check your understanding

1. Why is locality built into the LHV assumption as "A depends only on a and λ, never on b"?

2. In the CHSH proof, why does the bracketed expression always equal exactly ±2 for any fixed λ?

3. What does the measured value S ≈ 2.828 (matching the quantum prediction) actually demonstrate?

Practice before next lesson

Pick a specific λ and hidden-variable assignment: say A(a₁,λ)=+1, A(a₂,λ)=−1, B(b₁,λ)=+1, B(b₂,λ)=+1. Compute the bracketed CHSH term A(a₁,λ)[B(b₁,λ)+B(b₂,λ)] + A(a₂,λ)[B(b₁,λ)−B(b₂,λ)] by hand and confirm it comes out to exactly ±2, the way the lesson's proof claims for any assignment. Then flip one of your four choices and redo it — confirm you still land on ±2, never anything in between.
