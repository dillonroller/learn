The Schrödinger Equation

Lessons 1–3 were entirely about static pictures — what a state is, what happens the instant you measure it, what correlations entangled pairs produce. None of it said what a state does between measurements, while nothing is watching. This lesson answers that, and in doing so makes something you've been quietly promised since lesson 1 — that normalization always holds — into something actually proven, not just asserted, and exposes the real fault line at the center of the "measurement problem."

The equation

The (time-dependent) Schrödinger Equation iℏ d|ψ⟩/dt = H|ψ⟩, where H is the Hamiltonian — the operator corresponding to total energy — and ℏ is a fixed physical constant.

Read it as: the rate of change of the state vector is determined entirely by applying one fixed operator, H, to the current state. This is a first-order linear differential equation for |ψ⟩ — mechanically the same kind of object you'd meet solving dx/dt = kx, just for a vector in a complex Hilbert space instead of a real number.

Solving it: unitary time evolution

For a time-independent H, the equation has a clean, closed-form solution:

|ψ(t)⟩ = e^(−iHt/ℏ) |ψ(0)⟩  =:  U(t)|ψ(0)⟩

where e^(−iHt/ℏ) is defined the same way eˣ is for numbers — via its power series, now applied to an operator. U(t) has a crucial property: it's unitary, meaning U†(t)U(t) = I (its conjugate-transpose is its inverse).

Why unitarity is the whole point Unitary evolution automatically preserves the normalization ⟨ψ(t)|ψ(t)⟩ = 1 for every t — you can check this directly: ⟨ψ(t)|ψ(t)⟩ = ⟨ψ(0)|U†(t)U(t)|ψ(0)⟩ = ⟨ψ(0)|I|ψ(0)⟩ = ⟨ψ(0)|ψ(0)⟩ = 1. Lesson 1's normalization constraint |α|²+|β|²=1 wasn't just a rule imposed once at the start — the Schrödinger equation guarantees it stays true forever, automatically, for any state evolving this way. Unitary evolution is also reversible — since U†(t) = U(t)⁻¹ always exists, you can always run time backward and recover the exact earlier state, with nothing lost.

Two rules, and they don't agree with each other

The actual content of the "measurement problem" Quantum mechanics, as normally presented, has two separate rules for how a state changes, and they behave nothing alike:
  • Schrödinger evolution (this lesson): deterministic, reversible, continuous — |ψ(t)⟩ is a smooth, predictable function of t, and you can always undo it.
  • Measurement / the Born rule (lesson 1): probabilistic, irreversible, discontinuous — the state jumps to a definite basis vector, and there's no U(t) that undoes a collapse.
Nothing in the theory as usually stated says precisely when the smooth, deterministic rule stops applying and the abrupt, probabilistic one takes over — "when a measurement happens" is doing a lot of unexamined work. This is exactly what lesson 3's interpretations disagree about: many-worlds denies collapse ever really happens (everything is unitary evolution, forever, and "measurement" is really just entanglement with a measuring device); Copenhagen treats collapse as a genuine, separate physical process; Bohmian mechanics adds hidden trajectories underneath the wavefunction entirely. This lesson isn't settling that — it's making precise exactly what's in tension.

Worked example: a qubit that visibly changes even though its 0/1 odds don't

Take H = (ℏω/2) times the operator that sends |0⟩ ↦ |0⟩ and |1⟩ ↦ −|1⟩ (a standard, simple Hamiltonian — an energy "splitting" ω between the two basis states). Starting from |ψ(0)⟩ = (|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2, solving the Schrödinger equation gives:

|ψ(t)⟩ = (e^(−iωt/2)|0⟩ + e^(iωt/2)|1⟩) / √2

Measuring in the {|0⟩,|1⟩} basis: Born-rule probabilities are |e^(∓iωt/2)/√2|² = 1/2 for both outcomes, at every t — a complex phase always has magnitude 1, so it vanishes under the Born rule's squaring. It would be easy to conclude nothing is happening. But measure instead in the rotated basis |+⟩=(|0⟩+|1⟩)/√2, |−⟩=(|0⟩−|1⟩)/√2: working out ⟨+|ψ(t)⟩ gives probability cos²(ωt/2) for outcome +, genuinely oscillating between 0 and 1 as t increases. The state was changing the entire time — a fact only visible once you measure in a basis that's sensitive to the relative phase between |0⟩ and |1⟩.

Check your understanding

1. Why does U(t) being unitary guarantee normalization is preserved for all t?

2. What makes Schrödinger evolution "reversible" in a way that measurement is not?

3. In the worked example, why do the {|0⟩,|1⟩}-basis measurement probabilities stay at 1/2 and 1/2 for all t, even though the state is genuinely changing?

4. What does it mean that "nothing in the theory says precisely when Schrödinger evolution stops and measurement collapse takes over"?

Practice before next lesson

Using the worked example's |ψ(t)⟩, compute ⟨+|ψ(t)⟩ by hand at t such that ωt/2 = π/2 (i.e. ωt = π), and confirm the probability of measuring + comes out to cos²(π/2) = 0 — the state has rotated all the way to being certainly |−⟩ at that moment, purely from Schrödinger evolution, with no measurement involved anywhere in the calculation.
