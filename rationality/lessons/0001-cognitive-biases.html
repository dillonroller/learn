Cognitive Biases — Catching Your Own Mind

Logic lesson 12 was a catalog for spotting broken reasoning in someone else's argument. This lesson is the harder, more useful version: a catalog for spotting your own mind quietly cutting corners, in the moment, before you've said anything out loud. Most of the reasoning that goes wrong in an actual disagreement with someone you're close to isn't a formal fallacy at all — it's one of these four biases running underneath, unnoticed.

Confirmation bias — noticing what agrees with you, missing what doesn't You already believe your partner doesn't listen when you talk. So tonight, when they check their phone mid-conversation, it lands hard — proof. The three times this week they put the phone down and listened closely? Those didn't register as evidence of anything; they were just "normal," not worth counting. Confirmation bias isn't lying to yourself — it's an asymmetry in what gets noticed and remembered as evidence at all.
Motivated reasoning — working backward from the conclusion you want You want to believe the expensive thing you just bought was a good decision, so you find yourself listing reasons it was smart, and quietly not looking too hard at the reasons it wasn't. This is subtly different from confirmation bias: confirmation bias is about how you weigh evidence you already believe; motivated reasoning is reasoning driven by wanting a specific conclusion to be true, then reverse-engineering the justification. It's the mechanism behind most self-serving rationalizations.
Fundamental attribution error — character for them, circumstance for you They're 20 minutes late: "They're inconsiderate, they don't respect my time." You're 20 minutes late: "Traffic was awful, there was nothing I could do." Same action, opposite explanations — their behavior gets attributed to a fixed trait of character, yours gets attributed to circumstance. This one specific asymmetry is behind an enormous share of relationship arguments that escalate past the original issue.
Availability heuristic — vivid examples feel more likely than they are One dramatic argument three months ago is easy to recall in full detail — the exact words, where you were standing. A hundred ordinary, uneventful evenings since then don't come to mind nearly as easily, because nothing about them was memorable. If you judge "how often do we fight?" by how easily examples come to mind rather than actually counting, the vivid, rare event will feel far more representative than it is. This is exactly the setup for lesson 2's base rate problem.

Where these connect back to Logic

Biases and fallacies aren't the same thing, but they're closely related: a bias is a psychological tendency happening inside one mind; a fallacy is a flaw in an argument's content once it's spoken aloud. Confirmation bias is often what's underneath a hasty generalization — you generalize confidently because the confirming cases are the ones you noticed. Motivated reasoning frequently produces circular reasoning — assuming the conclusion you wanted all along, then finding it "confirmed." Knowing the bias behind a fallacy tells you something the fallacy name alone doesn't: why a specific person made that specific mistake right now.

Practice before next lesson
