Probabilistic & Bayesian Reasoning

Logic asked one question: is this argument valid, yes or no? Real life almost never hands you a clean yes/no — it hands you evidence, and asks how confident you should be. This lesson is about updating confidence correctly, in proportion to the evidence, instead of swinging between "certain it's fine" and "certain it's a disaster" based on whatever happened most recently — exactly the trap lesson 1's availability heuristic sets.

Bayes' theorem, in plain terms

Bayes' theorem is a rule for updating a belief when new evidence comes in. In symbols:

P(H|E) = P(E|H) × P(H) / P(E)

In words: your updated belief in a hypothesis H, given evidence E, depends on three things — how likely the evidence is if H is true, how likely H was before you saw the evidence (the prior), and how likely the evidence was at all, regardless of H. The part people consistently skip is the prior — and skipping it is the single most common way probabilistic reasoning goes wrong.

Base rate neglect — the classic failure

The test-accuracy problem A disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A test for it is 90% accurate — meaning it correctly flags 90% of people who have it, and correctly clears 90% of people who don't. You test positive. How likely is it you actually have the disease?

Most people's gut answer is "around 90%." The real answer is closer to 1%. Out of 1,000 people, roughly 1 actually has the disease and tests positive (0.9 people). But of the 999 who don't have it, 10% still test positive by error — that's about 100 false positives. So out of ~101 positive results, only about 1 is a true case. The test result is real evidence and should shift your belief upward — from 1-in-1,000 to roughly 1-in-100 — just nowhere near as far as gut instinct says, because the base rate (how rare the disease is to begin with) matters just as much as the test's accuracy.

This exact structure shows up constantly outside medicine. A friend mentions your partner was seen having lunch with an ex (the "positive test"). If cheating is genuinely rare in your relationship's actual track record (a low base rate), one ambiguous data point should move your belief a little — not flip it to certainty. Ignoring the base rate and reacting as if the evidence alone determines the answer is base rate neglect, and it's one of the most consistent gaps between formal probability and gut-level confidence.

Updating proportionally, not flipping a switch

The practical takeaway isn't the formula — it's the habit: treat new evidence as something that should shift your confidence by a proportionate amount, not snap it to 0% or 100%. "They forgot to text back once" is real evidence about reliability, but it's weak evidence, so it should nudge a strong prior (months of reliable behavior) only slightly. "They've forgotten to text back the last six times in a row" is much stronger evidence, and deserves a much bigger shift. The size of the update should track the strength of the evidence and how surprising it is given what you already knew — not the emotional intensity of the moment it arrived in.

Calibration — confidence should track being right

A well-calibrated reasoner who says "I'm 80% sure" should turn out to be right about 8 times out of 10 when they say that — not nearly always, and not barely more than half the time. Most people are systematically overconfident: their "I'm certain" moments turn out wrong more often than 0% of the time. Treating your own gut confidence as informative, but not infallible, and asking "how often am I right when I feel this sure?" is the practical, ongoing version of Bayesian updating — done without ever writing down a formula.

Practice before next lesson

Next time you feel suddenly, strongly certain about something involving another person's intentions, pause and ask: what's the base rate here (how often has this actually happened before), and how strong is this one piece of evidence really, on its own? Write down your before-and-after confidence as two numbers, even rough ones — the act of naming them is the whole exercise.
