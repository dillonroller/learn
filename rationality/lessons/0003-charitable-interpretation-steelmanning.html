Charitable Interpretation & Steelmanning

Logic lesson 12 taught you to spot a straw man — attacking a misrepresented, weakened version of someone's actual position. This lesson teaches the constructive flip side: actively doing the opposite, on purpose, before you respond to anyone — especially someone you're close to, where the stakes of getting it wrong are highest.

The principle of charity

Interpret the strongest reasonable version first When someone's statement is ambiguous, vague, or exaggerated, the principle of charity says: interpret it as the most reasonable, most defensible thing they could plausibly mean — not the most literal, most extreme, or easiest-to-dismiss reading. This isn't about being generous for its own sake. It's about actually engaging with what the person is trying to say, rather than a version of it you made up.

Steelmanning — the constructive opposite of a straw man

A straw man takes someone's actual position and inflates it into something extreme and easy to knock down. Steelmanning does the reverse on purpose: you take their position and restate it in its strongest, most charitable, most defensible form — sometimes even stronger than they stated it themselves — before you respond to it at all.

"You never help around the house" Uncharitable (strawman-able) reading: a literal universal claim — "not once, ever" — which is probably false and easy to dismiss with a single counterexample.

Steelmanned reading: "I've been feeling like I'm carrying more than my fair share lately, and I want that acknowledged." That's a claim about felt balance and acknowledgment, not a literal, falsifiable frequency count — and it's usually what's actually meant. Responding to the literal version ("that's not true, I did the dishes Tuesday") answers a claim nobody was actually making, and the real issue goes unaddressed.

Facts vs. values — know which disagreement you're in

Before steelmanning, it helps to know what kind of disagreement you're actually in. A disagreement about facts ("did you forget to call the plumber?") is resolvable with evidence — check the messages, settle it. A disagreement about values or feelings ("I don't feel like a priority to you") is not resolvable that way at all — there's no fact to check, and treating it like one (marshaling evidence, "proving" you're not wrong) misses the point entirely and usually escalates things. Steelmanning matters most exactly in the second kind, where the literal words are often standing in for something felt rather than something measurable.

The practical technique

Before responding to a charged statement, say it back in its strongest form and confirm it: "So what I'm hearing is [steelmanned version] — is that right?" This does two things at once: it forces you to actually construct the charitable reading instead of reacting to the literal one, and it gives the other person a chance to correct you if you got it wrong — which is strictly better than silently arguing against a version of their point they never intended.

Try it: find the steelman

For each statement, pick the genuinely charitable (steelmanned) reading, not the easiest one to dismiss.

Practice before next time

Next time someone says something charged or vague to you, try saying their steelmanned version back to them before responding to it at all. Notice whether the conversation goes differently than it usually does.
