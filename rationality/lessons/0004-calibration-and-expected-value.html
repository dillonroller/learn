Calibration & Expected Value

Lesson 2 ended with one paragraph on calibration — "80% sure should be right 80% of the time" — and left it there. This lesson turns that sentence into something you can actually compute: a number for how good your confidence is, and a number for whether a decision is worth making at all.

Expected value — putting a number on a bet

Expected value (EV) is the average outcome you'd get if you could replay a decision many times, weighted by how likely each outcome is:

EV = Σ  p(outcome) × value(outcome)

Worked example: a friend offers you a side gig that pays $2,000 if it works out, and costs you $500 in wasted time if it doesn't. You genuinely believe it has a 30% chance of working.

EV = (0.30 × $2,000) + (0.70 × −$500)
   = $600 − $350
   = +$250

Positive EV doesn't mean it will work out — 70% of the time it still won't, and you'll be out $500 that specific time. It means that if decisions like this come up repeatedly, saying yes to the positive-EV ones and no to the negative-EV ones wins on average. EV is a tool for not being ruled by whichever single outcome feels most vivid in the moment.

One correction: value isn't always dollars

Why $500 lost doesn't always equal $500 gained Plain EV silently assumes each dollar is worth the same regardless of who has it or how many they already have. That's often false: $500 lost when it's your rent money hurts far more than $500 gained as a bonus helps. Formal decision theory handles this with expected utility — replacing raw dollar amounts with how much each outcome is actually worth to you before averaging. This lesson won't build the full utility-theory machinery (that's genuinely a semester of its own), but keep the correction in your pocket: before trusting a plain-dollar EV calculation, ask whether the loss and the gain are actually symmetric in how much they'd matter to you. If they're not, weight the worse outcome harder than the raw number suggests.

Calibration, quantitatively

Lesson 2 defined calibration in words. Here's the actual check: collect every prediction you made at a given confidence level, and see how many came true.

A calibration curve, worked Say over the last few months you made 10 predictions where you felt "70% sure," and 20 where you felt "90% sure." If you're well-calibrated, about 7 of the first group and about 18 of the second group should have come true — not exactly, but close. If all 20 of your "90% sure" predictions came true, you're not extremely accurate — you're underconfident: your gut is quietly more certain than "90%" and you should be saying "99%." If only 12 of the 20 came true, you're overconfident: your gut says "90%" for things that are really more like a coin flip with a thumb on the scale.

The habit this builds on: most people never check. They remember the dramatic misses and forget the routine hits (or vice versa), which is confirmation bias operating on your own track record. Writing predictions down with a number, before you know the outcome, is what makes an honest check possible at all.

The Brier score — scoring calibration, not just being right

Brier Score For a set of N predictions, where p is your stated probability (0 to 1) and o is the actual outcome (1 if it happened, 0 if it didn't): 
Brier = (1/N) × Σ (p − o)²
Lower is better. 0 is a perfect forecaster; 0.25 is what you get by always guessing 50%; 1 is maximally, confidently wrong every time.

Why square the difference instead of just averaging "was I right": squaring punishes overconfidence specifically. Saying "99% sure" and being wrong costs you (0.99−0)² ≈ 0.98 — nearly the maximum possible penalty — while saying "60% sure" and being wrong only costs (0.60−0)² = 0.36. A scoring rule that did this makes honesty the optimal strategy: you can't game a low Brier score by just always claiming near-certainty, because being confidently wrong is punished far harder than being modestly wrong. This is also why a well-calibrated "I don't know, maybe 55%" is a genuinely better answer than a false "I'm sure" — not just more honest, but literally scored better over time.

When this toolkit is actually worth reaching for

Not every decision deserves a spreadsheet. The boundary that matters:

  • Reach for EV/calibration when: the decision repeats (so "on average" is a meaningful frame), the stakes are large enough that being systematically wrong compounds, or you notice your gut has been swinging between "certain it's fine" and "certain it's a disaster" — exactly lesson 2's original trap. Writing down a number forces the swing into the open.
  • Skip it when: the question is really about values, not facts (lesson 3) — EV can't tell you whether to prioritize a friend's feelings over being technically right, because that's not a probability question. It's also overkill for genuinely trivial, one-off, low-stakes calls, where the time spent computing costs more than the calculation could ever save you.

The skill isn't "always calculate EV." It's noticing, in the moment, which kind of decision you're in — the same diagnostic move lesson 3 taught for facts vs. values, now extended to "does this even call for a number."

Practice before next lesson

Over the next two weeks, write down five predictions you're genuinely uncertain about — an outcome at work, whether a plan falls through, anything with a real answer coming later — each with a number attached ("70% this happens"). When the outcomes land, check them against what you predicted. You're not looking for a perfect Brier score on five data points; you're building the habit lesson 2 asked for but never made concrete: naming your confidence before you know the answer.
